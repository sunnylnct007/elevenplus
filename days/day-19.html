📐 Maths (~20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 7/9 ÷ 14/27?
    3/2 = 1½. Flip and multiply: 7/9 × 27/14. Cancel: 7s and 9s simplify → 1×3/1×2 = 3/2.
  2. What is 15% of 15% of 400?
    9. Step 1: 15% of 400 = 60. Step 2: 15% of 60 = 9.
  3. Expand and simplify: (x + 3)(x + 5).
    x² + 8x + 15. FOIL: x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15.
  4. A line passes through (0, 2) and (3, 8). What is its gradient?
    2. Gradient = (8−2)/(3−0) = 6/3 = 2.
  5. Write 0.000045 in standard form.
    4.5 × 10⁻⁵. Move the decimal 5 places right to get 4.5, so the power is −5.

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A house price increased by 12% to £336,000. What was the original price?
    £300,000. £336,000 = 112% of original. Original = £336,000 ÷ 1.12 = £300,000.
  2. A cone has radius 6 cm and height 8 cm. Find its volume. (V = ⅓πr²h, π = 3.14)
    301.44 cm³. V = ⅓ × 3.14 × 36 × 8 = ⅓ × 904.32 = 301.44 cm³.
  3. In a survey of 80 people: 1/4 prefer tea, 3/8 prefer coffee, the rest prefer water. What percentage prefer water?
    37.5%. Tea: 1/4 = 2/8. Together tea+coffee: 5/8. Water = 3/8. As %: 3/8 × 100 = 37.5%.
  4. Two dice are rolled. What is the probability of a total of 7?
    1/6. Combinations totalling 7: (1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1) = 6 outcomes out of 36. P = 6/36 = 1/6.
  5. A car depreciates by 15% each year. It cost £20,000 new. What is it worth after 2 years?
    £14,450. After year 1: £20,000 × 0.85 = £17,000. After year 2: £17,000 × 0.85 = £14,450.

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is the difference between assonance and consonance? Give one example of each.
    Assonance = repetition of vowel sounds: "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain." Consonance = repetition of consonant sounds within or at the end of words: "The rocks clacked and clicked." Both create musical effects in poetry and prose.
  2. Explain the term "unreliable narrator" and give an example.
    A narrator whose account cannot be fully trusted — due to limited knowledge, self-interest, or deliberate deception. A child narrator may be unreliable because they misunderstand what adults are doing. The effect: the reader must read between the lines.
  3. Rewrite using a non-finite clause opener: She was exhausted after the race, so she sat down immediately.
    "Exhausted after the race, she sat down immediately." The participial phrase opens the sentence with economy and elegance.
  4. What does perspicacious mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Having a ready insight into things; shrewdly observant and analytical. Example: "The perspicacious detective noticed, without being told, that the window had been opened from the inside."
  5. What is the effect of short, simple sentences in fiction? When should a writer use them?
    Short sentences create pace, tension, and impact. They draw the eye and make the reader pause. Best used at moments of shock ("She was dead."), fragmented thought under stress, or to end a scene with finality. Their power comes from contrast with longer sentences.

2. Comprehension — The Truth About Glass

Glass is one of the strangest materials in the world. It is not, strictly speaking, a solid. Scientists classify it as an amorphous solid — a material that has the disordered structure of a liquid but behaves like a solid. This is why very old windows, in ancient churches and cathedrals, appear thicker at the bottom than at the top. For centuries, the explanation offered was that glass flows downwards imperceptibly slowly over hundreds of years. This idea is almost certainly a myth. The real reason is that medieval glassmakers could not make perfectly uniform glass, and when panes were fitted, the thicker edge was typically placed at the bottom for stability. But the myth is so appealing, so wonderfully strange, that many people prefer it to the truth.
  1. What is an "amorphous solid"?
    A material with the disordered internal structure of a liquid but that behaves outwardly like a solid.
  2. What explanation was given for why old church windows appear thicker at the bottom?
    That glass flows very slowly downwards over hundreds of years, accumulating at the bottom of the pane.
  3. What is the real explanation?
    Medieval glassmakers couldn't make perfectly uniform glass, so when panes were fitted, the thicker edge was placed at the bottom for stability — the uneven thickness was there from the start, not caused by flow.
  4. What does "the myth is so appealing, so wonderfully strange" reveal about the writer's attitude?
    The writer is sympathetic to human preference for the myth, finding it understandable. "Wonderfully" shows genuine appreciation of the myth's imaginative power, suggesting that the desire for mystery is a natural human quality worth acknowledging.
  5. What broader point might the writer be making in the final sentence?
    Humans sometimes choose appealing stories over accurate facts. Imagination and wonder can be more compelling than rational explanation. This is not a criticism but an observation: we are storytelling creatures, drawn to mystery.

3. Writing task

Choose an ordinary object (a mirror, a key, a clock) and write a short piece that starts with the scientific truth about it — then explores the myths and meanings people have given it.

Use: a clear shift between the scientific and the mythological, precise vocabulary, and a final line that reflects on why humans need stories.

A mirror is, in scientific terms, a flat surface coated with a thin layer of metal that reflects light with minimal scattering. When you stand before one, you are seeing photons bounced back at you in less than a nanosecond. There is nothing more to it than physics. And yet. Mirrors have been believed, across centuries, to hold the souls of the dead, to tell the future, to reveal the true self that the face conceals. In fairy tales, they speak and deceive. In superstition, breaking one costs seven years. Physics cannot explain this. But then, physics was never what mirrors were really for.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Depreciation: A computer costs £800. It loses 20% of its value each year. How much is it worth after 3 years?
    £409.60. Year 1: £800 × 0.8 = £640. Year 2: £640 × 0.8 = £512. Year 3: £512 × 0.8 = £409.60.
  2. FOIL & substitution: Expand (x + 4)(x − 2). Then find the value when x = 5.
    x² + 2x − 8. When x = 5: 25 + 10 − 8 = 27.
  3. Probability: Three coins are flipped. What is the probability of getting exactly 2 heads?
    3/8. Possible outcomes with exactly 2 heads: HHT, HTH, THH = 3 outcomes. Total outcomes = 2³ = 8. P = 3/8.

💬 Word of the Day

empirical
adjective
Based on observation or experience rather than theory or pure logic. From Greek empeiria — experience.
"The empirical evidence showed clearly that glass in old windows had been uneven from the moment it was made."
