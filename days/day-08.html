Day 8

Square Numbers, Sequences & Grandmother Rosa

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 14 × 14?
    Answer: 196. Use (14 × 10) + (14 × 4) = 140 + 56 = 196. Remember: 14² = 196 is a useful square number to know.
  2. What is 5/8 of 200?
    Answer: 125. Find 1/8: 200 ÷ 8 = 25. Multiply by 5: 25 × 5 = 125.
  3. Write 45% as a fraction in its simplest form.
    Answer: 9/20. 45% = 45/100. Divide top and bottom by 5: 9/20.
  4. What is the perimeter of a regular hexagon with sides of 7 cm?
    Answer: 42 cm. A regular hexagon has 6 equal sides. Perimeter = 6 × 7 = 42 cm.
  5. What are the first five square numbers?
    Answer: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25. These are 1², 2², 3², 4², 5².

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A school trip costs £15 per child. 28 children go on the trip. What is the total cost?
    Answer: £420. 15 × 28 = (15 × 20) + (15 × 8) = 300 + 120 = 420.
  2. The temperature at midnight is −6°C. By noon it has risen by 14°C. What is the temperature at noon?
    Answer: 8°C. −6 + 14 = 8°C.
  3. A pie chart shows how a class spends its school day. The school day is 6 hours and 30% of time is spent on Maths. How many minutes are spent on Maths?
    Answer: 108 minutes. Total time = 6 hours = 360 minutes. 30% of 360 = 360 ÷ 10 × 3 = 108 minutes.
  4. A number pattern: 2, 6, 18, 54, … What is the rule? What are the next two terms?
    Rule: multiply by 3 each time. Next two terms: 54 × 3 = 162, 162 × 3 = 486.
  5. A car park charges £1.20 for the first hour and 80p for each additional hour. How much does it cost to park for 4 hours?
    Answer: £3.60. First hour: £1.20. Additional 3 hours: 3 × 80p = £2.40. Total = £1.20 + £2.40 = £3.60.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. Which is the correct spelling?
    A) recieve    B) receive    C) receve
    Answer: B) receive. Remember: “i before e except after c” — re-C-eive has the e before i. Other words: perceive, deceive, conceive.
  2. Punctuate this sentence correctly:
    after the match which lasted two hours the exhausted players collapsed on the grass
    Answer: After the match, which lasted two hours, the exhausted players collapsed on the grass. The relative clause “which lasted two hours” needs commas either side. The sentence starts with a subordinate clause, so a comma follows it too.
  3. Expand this noun phrase with at least three extra words:
    the house
    Example: “the crumbling, ivy-covered house at the end of the lane.” A noun phrase can be expanded with adjectives, prepositional phrases, and determiners.
  4. What does ambiguous mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Ambiguous means open to more than one interpretation — unclear or having more than one possible meaning. Example: “The instruction was ambiguous, so nobody was sure whether to turn left or right.”
  5. What is the difference between first person and third person narrative? Give an example of each.
    First person uses “I/we” — the narrator is a character: e.g. “I crept through the doorway.” Third person uses “he/she/they” — the narrator is outside the story: e.g. “She crept through the doorway.” Third person can be limited (one character’s viewpoint) or omniscient (all-knowing).

2. Short comprehension

Nobody in our village had ever left and come back. That was the unspoken rule — leave if you must, but don’t return and expect things to be the same. So when Grandmother Rosa stepped off the bus after forty years away, dragging a battered suitcase and wearing a yellow hat that nobody recognised, the whole street went quiet. My mother gripped my hand tightly. I looked up at her face and saw something I had never seen there before: uncertainty. Grandmother Rosa looked at each house in turn, slowly, as if taking inventory. Then her eyes settled on us. She smiled — a slow, deliberate smile — and said: “Well. I see you kept the garden.”
  1. How long had Grandmother Rosa been away?
    Answer: Forty years.
  2. What does the narrator notice on the mother’s face? Why is this significant?
    The narrator notices uncertainty — an emotion they have never seen before. This is significant because it suggests the mother is usually confident or in control, so this moment tells us how unsettling Rosa’s return is, even for adults.
  3. What is “the unspoken rule” and why do you think such a rule exists in the village?
    The unspoken rule is that people who leave should not return expecting things to be unchanged. It likely exists because communities change, relationships fade, and returning stirs up the past. It may also suggest the village is close-knit or traditional, where change is resisted.
  4. What is the effect of Rosa’s final line: “Well. I see you kept the garden.”?
    The line is understated and mysterious — after forty years away, her first comment is about the garden, not the family. This could suggest she is guarded, humorous, or hiding deeper emotion. The brevity of “Well.” creates a dramatic pause. The remark implies she has been observing carefully and that she remembers specific details from before she left.
  5. This story is told in the first person. What effect does this have on the reader?
    First-person narration creates intimacy — we experience the scene through a child’s eyes, which makes adult emotions seem more powerful because the narrator doesn’t fully understand them. It also limits what we know: we only see what the narrator sees, which creates mystery around Rosa’s feelings and intentions.

3. Short writing task

Write a short paragraph or story opening (about 8–10 sentences):

“Later that evening, Grandmother Rosa sits with the family for the first time. Write the scene. What is said? What is left unsaid? How does the atmosphere feel?”

Try to use: first person narrator (I/we), actions and body language to show how characters feel (not just telling us), and a mix of dialogue and description.

Example answer:
We sat around the kitchen table with the good china out, which Mum only brought down for funerals and Christmas. Nobody had said as much, but we were all trying. Grandmother Rosa held her cup with both hands and looked at the photographs on the wall — slowly, one by one, without commenting. “Your father was a good man,” she said at last. Mum made a small sound that wasn’t quite agreement. I kept my eyes on my plate. Outside, the garden was dark, and somewhere a fox was calling. Rosa set down her cup precisely in the centre of its saucer. “You’ve all grown,” she said, and it sounded less like a compliment and more like a verdict.

Check for: (1) first-person narration: “I”, “we”, “Mum” (2) show don’t tell: “made a small sound that wasn’t quite agreement” (3) dialogue mixed with description.

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking.

  1. Percentages: A jacket originally costs £80. It is reduced by 15% in a sale. A week later, the sale price is reduced by a further 10%. What is the final price?
    First reduction: 15% of £80 = £12. Sale price = £68. Second reduction: 10% of £68 = £6.80. Final price = £68 − £6.80 = £61.20. (Note: this is NOT the same as a 25% reduction!)
  2. Square numbers pattern: The sum of the first n odd numbers always equals n². Verify this for n = 4 and n = 5. Then find the sum of the first 10 odd numbers without adding them all up.
    n=4: 1+3+5+7 = 16 = 4² ✓. n=5: 1+3+5+7+9 = 25 = 5² ✓. First 10 odd numbers: sum = 10² = 100. (No need to add them all!)
  3. Geometric sequence: A culture of bacteria doubles every hour. At 9 am there are 50 bacteria. How many are there at 2 pm?
    From 9 am to 2 pm = 5 hours. Bacteria doubles 5 times: 50 × 2&sup5; = 50 × 32 = 1,600 bacteria.

Word of the Day

poignant
/ POY-nyent /  —  adjective
Evoking a keen sense of sadness or regret, often mixed with tenderness or beauty. Something poignant touches the emotions in a quiet, deep way — not loud grief, but a feeling that lingers.
“There was something poignant about Rosa’s first comment being about the garden — as if forty years could be contained in the simple fact of its survival.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using poignant to describe a moment between two people who haven’t seen each other in a long time. What makes a reunion poignant rather than simply happy?
