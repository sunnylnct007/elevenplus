Day 12

Simultaneous Equations, Sequences & The Lighthouse

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 345 × 8?
    Answer: 2,760. 300 × 8 = 2,400; 40 × 8 = 320; 5 × 8 = 40. Sum = 2,760.
  2. Simplify 24/36.
    Answer: 2/3. HCF of 24 and 36 = 12. 24 ÷ 12 = 2, 36 ÷ 12 = 3.
  3. What is 40% of 3.5 km in metres?
    3.5 km = 3,500 m. 40% × 3,500 = 1,400 m.
  4. What are the first three multiples of 11?
    Answer: 11, 22, 33.
  5. A piece of string is 2.4 m. How many 30 cm pieces can be cut?
    2.4 m = 240 cm. 240 ÷ 30 = 8 pieces.

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. 2 apples and 3 pears cost £2.05. 1 apple and 2 pears cost £1.20. Find the price of one apple.
    Let a = apple price, p = pear price. From equation 2: a + 2p = 1.20 → a = 1.20 − 2p. Substitute into equation 1: 2(1.20−2p) + 3p = 2.05 → 2.40 − 4p + 3p = 2.05 → p = 0.35. So a = 1.20 − 0.70 = 50p. Pear = 35p.
  2. A cylinder has radius 5 cm and height 12 cm. What is its volume? (Use π = 3.14)
    V = π × r² × h = 3.14 × 25 × 12 = 942 cm³.
  3. In a bag: 5 red, 7 blue, 3 green. What is P(not blue)?
    Total = 15. Not blue = 15 − 7 = 8. P(not blue) = 8/15.
  4. At 8:45 am, a train is 247 km from the station. It travels at 65 km/h. What time does it arrive?
    Time = 247 ÷ 65 = 3.8 hours = 3 h 48 min. Arrival = 8:45 + 3 h 48 min = 12:33 pm.
  5. Find the rule and next term: 100, 50, 25, 12.5, ___
    Rule: divide by 2 each time. Next term = 12.5 ÷ 2 = 6.25.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is alliteration? Give an example and explain why a writer might use it.
    Alliteration = repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words. Example: “The dark, damp dungeon dripped with dread.” Effects: creates rhythm, reinforces mood (harsh d-sounds = menace), makes writing memorable.
  2. Add a fronted adverbial and a relative clause to improve:
    The dog ran across the garden.
    Example: “In a burst of sudden energy, the dog that had been asleep for hours ran across the garden.” Fronted adverbial: “In a burst of sudden energy,” Relative clause: “that had been asleep for hours.”
  3. Write a sentence using parenthesis (a pair of dashes), then repeat it using brackets.
    Dashes: The journey — which took nearly four hours — was worth every minute. Brackets: The journey (which took nearly four hours) was worth every minute. Both add extra information that could be removed without breaking the sentence.
  4. What does melancholy mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Melancholy = a deep, gentle sadness, often without a clear reason. Example: “On autumn evenings, the empty park had a quiet melancholy about it.”
  5. What is pathetic fallacy? Give an example.
    Pathetic fallacy = using weather or environment to reflect a character’s emotions. Example: “As she left the funeral, rain began to fall.” The weather mirrors or creates mood.

2. Short comprehension

The lighthouse had stood on the cliff for over two hundred years. It had watched wars begin and end, storms wreck and spare, generations of fishing boats leave before dawn and return, or not return, by nightfall. It did not judge. It simply turned its light across the dark water — once, and again, and again — a steady pulse that said: here is the edge. Here is the danger. Here, still, is something solid. Now it was decommissioned. A modern navigation buoy bobbed somewhere out at sea, and the lighthouse would be converted into holiday flats. A sign on the door said viewings by appointment.
  1. What has the lighthouse witnessed over two hundred years?
    Wars beginning and ending; storms wrecking ships and sparing them; boats leaving before dawn and sometimes not returning.
  2. What is the effect of “or not return” in the passage?
    The phrase is placed after a pause (comma), making the reader slow down. The quiet, understated addition carries enormous weight — it acknowledges death and loss without dramatising it. The brevity is what makes it moving.
  3. Analyse “here is the edge. Here is the danger. Here, still, is something solid.”
    The anaphora (repeated “here”) creates rhythmic urgency — like the lighthouse beam itself. The three sentences build: edge (physical), danger (warning), something solid (reassurance). The structure mirrors the lighthouse’s function: warning, then comfort.
  4. How does the final sentence change the mood of the passage?
    It is deliberately flat and bathetic — the grand, stoic lighthouse reduced to a “viewings by appointment” notice. The contrast between its two centuries of vital purpose and its fate as holiday flats is the source of the passage’s sadness. The prose deflates from lyrical to bureaucratic.
  5. What theme does the writer explore?
    Loss, change, the replacement of the old with the new, and what is lost when the useful becomes obsolete. Also the contrast between grandeur and the mundane.

3. Short writing task

Write a short piece (about 8–10 sentences):

“Write about a building or place that has outlived its original purpose. Use personification, contrast between past and present, and a final line that lands with quiet sadness or irony.”

Example answer (an old post office):
It used to know the weight of things. Letters, parcels, small packages of hope and grief — it had held them all before sending them on their way. The queues went out the door on the day the welfare cheques arrived. Now it sells artisan bread and scented candles. The original counter is still there, behind the coffee machine, glass-topped, waiting as if someone might yet slide a letter across it. Nobody does.

Check for: (1) personification: “know the weight” (2) past vs present contrast: queues vs coffee shop (3) quiet final sentence: “Nobody does.”

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking.

  1. Simultaneous equations: 3 coffees and 2 teas cost £8.50. 1 coffee and 4 teas cost £7.50. Find the cost of each.
    3c + 2t = 8.50 (eq1), c + 4t = 7.50 (eq2). Multiply eq2 by 3: 3c + 12t = 22.50. Subtract eq1: 10t = 14.00, t = £1.40. Then c = 7.50 − 4(1.40) = 7.50 − 5.60 = £1.90.
  2. Probability (combined): A bag has 4 red and 6 blue balls. One ball is drawn, noted, and replaced. What is the probability that both the first and second draws are red?
    P(red first) = 4/10 = 2/5. P(red second) = 4/10 = 2/5 (replaced, so same). P(both red) = 2/5 × 2/5 = 4/25.
  3. Speed & time: A train travels 247 km at 65 km/h (the problem from today). Halfway through the journey it slows to 52 km/h for a 30-minute delay, then resumes at 65 km/h. How much later does it arrive?
    The delay is 30 minutes, regardless of the speed change (the delay is a stop, not a speed reduction). So the train arrives exactly 30 minutes later than the original 12:33 pm → 13:03.

Word of the Day

stoic
/ STOH-ik /  —  adjective / noun
Enduring pain, difficulty, or hardship without complaining or showing emotion. A stoic person accepts what cannot be changed and faces adversity with quiet strength. (Originally from the ancient Greek philosophical school of Stoicism.)
“The lighthouse was stoic in the face of every storm — it did not judge, it did not waver; it simply turned its light across the dark water, again and again.”
