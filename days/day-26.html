Maths

Part 1 — Core skills warm-up
  1. If y = x² − 3x + 2, what is y when x = 4?
    y = 6. y = 16 − 12 + 2 = 6.
  2. What is 7/12 + 5/8? Give your answer as a mixed number.
    1 5/24. LCM of 12 and 8 = 24. 14/24 + 15/24 = 29/24 = 1 5/24.
  3. A ratio is 4 : 9. If the larger part is 63, what is the smaller part?
    28. 1 part = 63 ÷ 9 = 7. Smaller part = 4 × 7 = 28.
  4. Solve: x² = 81. Give both solutions.
    x = 9 or x = −9. Always remember the negative root when solving x² = k.
  5. Find the median of: 8, 15, 3, 22, 7, 19, 11.
    11. Ordered: 3, 7, 8, 11, 15, 19, 22. The middle (4th of 7) = 11.
Part 2 — Word problems & exam-style practice
  1. A rectangle has a perimeter of 54 cm and its length is twice its width. Find the area.
    162 cm². Let width = w, length = 2w. Perimeter: 2(2w + w) = 54 → 6w = 54 → w = 9 cm, l = 18 cm. Area = 9 × 18 = 162 cm².
  2. There are 100 lottery tickets and you buy 5. What is the probability of winning?
    1/20 (= 0.05). P = 5/100 = 1/20.
  3. At noon the temperature is 14°C. By midnight it has dropped 19°C. What is the temperature at midnight?
    −5°C. 14 − 19 = −5°C.
  4. Maya reads 40 pages on day 1, increasing by 5 pages per day. Her book has 360 pages. On which day does she finish?
    Day 7. Cumulative totals: Day 1: 40, Day 2: 85, Day 3: 135, Day 4: 190, Day 5: 250, Day 6: 315, Day 7: 385. She reaches 360 during day 7. ✓
  5. A 10 m ladder rests against a wall with its base 6 m from the wall. How high up the wall does it reach?
    8 m. h² = 10² − 6² = 100 − 36 = 64. h = √64 = 8 m.
h² = 10² − 6² = 64 → h = 8 m

English

Part 1 — SPaG & vocabulary
  1. What is bathos? Give an example.
    Bathos is an abrupt, jarring drop from the grand or serious to the trivial — used for comic or ironic effect. E.g. 'She had survived war, famine, and the collapse of three marriages — but she could not open the jar of pickles.' The fall to the mundane is deliberately deflating.
  2. What is the difference between implicit and explicit meaning?
    Explicit = stated directly in the text (you can quote it exactly). Implicit = implied — you must infer it from word choices or what is left unsaid. E.g. 'He was terrified' is explicit; 'His hands were shaking' implies fear.
  3. Improve this weak opener: It was a cold day and Sarah walked to school.
    Example improvement: 'The cold that morning was the kind that gets into your coat before you have even left the house.' Show the cold rather than state it; avoid opening with 'It was' and weak conjunctions.
  4. What does equivocal mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Equivocal means having more than one possible meaning; deliberately ambiguous or uncommitted. E.g. 'The minister gave an equivocal answer that satisfied nobody and committed him to nothing.'
  5. What is the purpose and effect of a cliffhanger ending?
    A cliffhanger ends a section at a moment of unresolved tension — a sudden revelation, threat, or shift. It compels the reader to continue, creates suspense, and gives the writer control over pace and reader engagement.
Part 2 — Comprehension: The Wood Wide Web
Scientists have recently confirmed what many people have long suspected: trees communicate. Not with words, of course, but through a vast underground network of fungi — sometimes called the 'wood wide web' — through which they exchange water, carbon, and chemical signals. Older, larger trees ('mother trees') appear to support younger saplings by sending them nutrients. When a tree is attacked by insects, it can release chemical warnings that neighbouring trees detect and respond to by producing defensive compounds. Whether this constitutes 'language' in any meaningful sense is debated. But it raises uncomfortable questions about where we draw the line between a living system that responds and one that thinks.
  1. What is the 'wood wide web'?
    A vast underground network of fungi through which trees exchange water, carbon, and chemical signals.
  2. What do 'mother trees' do?
    They support younger saplings by sending them nutrients through the fungal network.
  3. How do trees respond when attacked by insects?
    They release chemical warnings; neighbouring trees detect these and produce defensive compounds in response.
  4. Why does the writer say this raises 'uncomfortable questions'?
    It blurs the boundary between a system that responds mechanically and one that 'thinks' — challenging the assumption that only animals with nervous systems can communicate meaningfully.
  5. What is the tone, and how does the writer avoid overstating the claim?
    Careful and measured. Hedging language — 'appear to', 'whether this constitutes… is debated' — keeps the writer from claiming more than can be proved. The final sentence is an open question, not a declaration.
Part 3 — Writing task
Write a short piece that begins with a scientific fact about the natural world and develops it into a reflection on what it means for us. Use: precise scientific language, a moment where you move beyond facts into meaning, and a question or reflection as your final line.
The giant sequoia cannot reproduce without fire. Its cones are sealed with resin and will only open after the intense heat of a forest blaze melts the seal and clears the ground of competing plants. To survive, it must first survive destruction. There are people like this — who do not flourish in comfortable conditions, who need the clearing, the stripping away, the thing that most organisms cannot survive. We tend to think of resilience as endurance: holding on. But for the sequoia, resilience is release: letting the fire come, and trusting that what follows will be growth. Whether that is comfort or challenge depends, I think, on where you are in the cycle.

Example: 'The passage raises a troubling question: could trees, through their chemical signals, be considered sentient in some minimal sense?'
