Maths

Part 1 — Core skills warm-up
  1. Solve: x² − 5x + 6 = 0.
    x = 2 or x = 3. Factorise: (x − 2)(x − 3) = 0. Check: 4 − 10 + 6 = 0 ✓ and 9 − 15 + 6 = 0 ✓.
  2. What is the gradient and y-intercept of the line y = 3x − 7?
    Gradient = 3; y-intercept = −7. The line is in the form y = mx + c. m = 3 (steepness), c = −7 (where the line crosses the y-axis).
  3. A bag has red and blue counters in the ratio 5 : 3. There are 40 counters in total. How many are red?
    25 red counters. Total parts = 5 + 3 = 8. One part = 40 ÷ 8 = 5. Red = 5 × 5 = 25.
  4. What is 3.6 × 10⁴ + 2.4 × 10³? Give your answer in standard form.
    3.84 × 10⁴. 3.6 × 10⁴ = 36,000. 2.4 × 10³ = 2,400. Sum = 38,400 = 3.84 × 10⁴.
  5. P(A) = 0.3; P(B) = 0.45; A and B are mutually exclusive. What is P(A or B)?
    0.75. Mutually exclusive means they cannot both happen at once, so P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) = 0.3 + 0.45 = 0.75.
Part 2 — Word problems & exam-style practice
  1. A sphere has a radius of 3 cm. Find its volume. (V = 4/3 × π × r³, use π = 3.14)
    113.04 cm³. V = 4/3 × 3.14 × 3³ = 4/3 × 3.14 × 27 = 4/3 × 84.78 = 113.04 cm³.
Sphere: V = 4/3 πr³
  1. A company's profit is £120,000 in Year 1. It grows at 8% compound interest per year. What is the profit in Year 3?
    £139,968. Year 2: £120,000 × 1.08 = £129,600. Year 3: £129,600 × 1.08 = £139,968.
  2. A parallelogram has base 11 cm and perpendicular height 7 cm. What is its area?
    77 cm². Area = base × perpendicular height = 11 × 7 = 77 cm².
  3. The bearing from Town A to Town B is 065°. What is the bearing from Town B back to Town A?
    245°. The back bearing = 065° + 180° = 245°. (Add 180°; if the result exceeds 360°, subtract 360°.)
Back bearing = 065° + 180° = 245°
  1. Prove algebraically that the sum of any three consecutive integers is always a multiple of 3.
    Let the integers be n, n+1, n+2.
    Sum = n + (n+1) + (n+2)
        = 3n + 3
        = 3(n+1)
    Since 3(n+1) is divisible by 3 for all integers n, the sum is always a multiple of 3. ✓ QED

English

Part 1 — SPaG & vocabulary
  1. What is polysyndeton? What is asyndeton? Give one example of each.
    Polysyndeton Using more conjunctions than necessary. 'She ran and jumped and laughed and shouted.' — the repeated 'and' builds breathless, accumulating energy.
    Asyndeton Omitting conjunctions for speed. 'She ran, jumped, laughed, shouted.' — the absence creates rapid, urgent pace.
    Both are deliberate rhetorical choices — not errors.
  2. What is a litotes? Give two examples.
    Litotes = understatement using a double negative or mild affirmative to convey the opposite. Examples: 'Not bad at all' (= very good). 'It's not the warmest day' (= it's very cold). 'He's no fool' (= he is quite clever). Common in British understatement and irony.
  3. What is a zeugma? Give an example.
    Zeugma uses one word (usually a verb) to govern two different objects in a surprising way, creating a comic or sharp effect. E.g. 'She left the party and all my hopes behind.' The verb 'left' governs 'the party' (literal) and 'my hopes' (figurative). Another: 'He lost his keys and his temper.' Often used for wit.
  4. What does inimitable mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Inimitable means so distinctive or excellent as to be impossible to copy; utterly unique. E.g. 'The author's inimitable style — combining dark humour with profound sadness — has never been successfully replicated.'
  5. What does catharsis mean in the context of literature or drama?
    Catharsis (from Greek: 'purging') is the emotional release or relief experienced by an audience at the end of a powerful piece of drama or literature — particularly tragedy. Aristotle described it as the purging of pity and fear through watching tragedy. After a cathartic ending, the audience feels emotionally spent and yet somehow cleansed. Shakespeare's tragedies often aim for this effect.
Part 2 — Comprehension: The Burning of the Fears
On the last day of term, Miss Osei did something she had never done before: she asked each pupil to write down, on a piece of paper, one thing they were afraid of. Not a spider or a thunderstorm — something real. Something that kept them awake. She collected the papers, unread, and placed them in a tin. Then she lit a candle and burned them, one by one, in the fireproof tray on her desk. Nobody spoke. The papers curled and blackened and were gone in less than a minute. Some children cried. Some watched very carefully. When the last one was ash, she looked up and said: "You are not your fears. They are just weather — they come and they go." Then she dismissed the class and nobody spoke in the corridor. They were all thinking about the smoke.
  1. What unusual thing does Miss Osei do, and what are the pupils asked to write?
    She asks each pupil to write down something genuinely frightening — not a trivial fear but something 'real' that keeps them awake. She then collects the papers unread and burns them one by one.
  2. Why is the detail that she read the papers 'unread' significant?
    It means the fears remained private — the act was for the pupils' benefit, not for Miss Osei's knowledge. The trust implied (she will not read or judge) is what makes the gesture powerful. It creates a space of genuine vulnerability without exposure.
  3. Analyse the metaphor 'They are just weather — they come and they go.'
    The metaphor compares fears to weather — unpredictable, beyond our control, but impermanent. Like weather, fears cannot always be prevented or reasoned away, yet they pass. The metaphor is comforting without being dismissive: it acknowledges that fears are real while insisting they are not permanent. The simplicity of the language suits the audience of children.
  4. What is the effect of 'They were all thinking about the smoke' as the final sentence?
    The sentence is understated and resonant. The smoke is literal (from the burning papers) and symbolic — the remnant of something destroyed. The children's collective, unspoken thought creates a moment of shared contemplation. It ends on a concrete image rather than an emotion, leaving the feeling implicit and trusting the reader to feel the weight without being told.
  5. How does the writer create a sense of ceremony and significance?
    Through the unusual, deliberate act (burning fears is ritualistic); collective silence ('Nobody spoke'); careful description of papers curling and blackening; Miss Osei only looking up after the last paper is ash; varied responses ('some cried, some watched carefully'); the quiet aftermath in the corridor. The measured prose mirrors the solemnity — the scene is made to feel almost sacred.
Part 3 — Writing task
Choose ONE:

Option A (Narrative): Write the opening of a story set in an unusual or unexpected place. Establish character, atmosphere, and a sense of what might happen next — all within 10–12 sentences.

Option B (Reflective): Write a short piece about something you have changed your mind about. Use: anaphora, tricolon, rhetorical questions, contrast, and a strong final line.
Option A (a waiting room):
The waiting room had been there longer than anyone could remember. Its chairs were a shade of green that had once had a name but no longer did. A notice on the wall said "Please take a number" but there were no numbers, and there was no counter to take them to. In the corner, a plant had been growing so long it had reached the ceiling and begun to grow back down. The people waiting did not speak to one another. They read old magazines and looked at their watches and waited. What they were waiting for was unclear. When you asked, they looked at you with mild surprise — as if waiting was simply what one did here, and asking why was slightly embarrassing. I had come to find my grandfather. I had been told he was in the waiting room. I sat down and picked up a magazine. It was dated 1987.

Option B (on silence):
I used to think silence was empty. I used to fill it immediately — with music, with talk, with the blue light of a screen. I was afraid of what silence might contain. I know now that I had it exactly wrong. It is the silence that contains everything: the thought you have been avoiding, the feeling you have been running from, the idea that has been waiting quietly for the noise to stop. Silence is not absence. It is the fullest thing there is. How strange, that we spend so much effort filling the one thing that could teach us the most. I have changed my mind about silence. I am still learning to change my habits.

Word of the Day

tacit
Meaning: understood or implied without being directly stated; silent but real.
Example: 'There was a tacit agreement among the children not to speak about what had happened in the classroom — some things are understood without words.'
Challenge: Think of a rule or understanding in your own life that is tacit — something everyone follows but nobody has ever said aloud. Describe it in one sentence without naming it directly.
