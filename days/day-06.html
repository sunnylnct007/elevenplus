Maths

~20 minutes
Part 1 — Core Skills Warm-up

Answer these quickly — mentally or with brief working:

  1. What is 11 × 15?
    Answer: 165
    Split method: (11 × 10) + (11 × 5) = 110 + 55 = 165.
  2. What is the HCF (Highest Common Factor) of 24 and 36?
    Answer: 12
    List the factor pairs for each number and find the highest factor that appears on both lists.
    Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24.
    Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36.
    The highest factor on both lists is 12.
    Factors of 24
    1 2 3 4 6 8 12 24
    Factors of 36
    1 2 3 4 6 9 12 18 36
    12 Shared factors are highlighted — the highest shared factor is 12.
  3. Write 0.6 as a fraction in its simplest form.
    Answer: 3/5
    0.6 = 6/10. Simplify by dividing top and bottom by 2: 6 ÷ 2 = 3, 10 ÷ 2 = 5. So 0.6 = 3/5.
  4. What is √49?
    Answer: 7
    √49 = 7 because 7 × 7 = 49. The square root asks: “which number multiplied by itself gives 49?”
  5. What do the angles inside a quadrilateral add up to?
    Answer: 360°
    Any quadrilateral (4-sided shape) has interior angles that sum to exactly 360°. You can see why by splitting a quadrilateral into two triangles — each triangle has 180°, so together 2 × 180 = 360°.
Part 2 — Word Problems & Exam-Style Practice

Show clear working for each question:

  1. A recipe uses 320 g of flour to make a dish for 4 people. How much flour is needed for 7 people?
    Answer: 560 g
    For 1 person: 320 ÷ 4 = 80 g.
    For 7 people: 80 × 7 = 560 g.
  2. A coat costs £48 and is reduced by 25% in a sale. What is the sale price?
    Answer: £36
    25% of £48 = £48 ÷ 4 = £12.
    Sale price = £48 − £12 = £36.
    Or: 75% of £48 = 0.75 × 48 = £36.
  3. The mean of 5 numbers is 14. Four of the numbers are 11, 15, 18, and 12. What is the fifth number?
    Answer: 14
    Total of all 5 numbers = mean × count = 14 × 5 = 70.
    Sum of the four known numbers = 11 + 15 + 18 + 12 = 56.
    Fifth number = 70 − 56 = 14.
  4. A map has a scale of 1:50,000. Two towns are 6 cm apart on the map. What is the real distance in kilometres?
    Answer: 3 km
    Real distance = 6 × 50,000 = 300,000 cm.
    Convert: 300,000 cm ÷ 100 = 3,000 m ÷ 1,000 = 3 km.
  5. A number is multiplied by 6, then 14 is added, giving 62. What was the original number?
    Answer: 8
    Work backwards using inverse operations:
    62 − 14 = 48,  then 48 ÷ 6 = 8.
    Check: 8 × 6 = 48, 48 + 14 = 62. ✓

    ×6 →
    ×6
    +14 →
    62
    reverse: −14 then ÷6

English

~20–25 minutes
Part 1 — SPaG & Vocabulary
  1. What word class is “tired” in this sentence?
    The tired children fell asleep immediately.
    Answer: adjective
    “Tired” describes the noun “children” — it tells us what the children are like. Adjectives modify nouns. Other clues: you could ask “what kind of children?” and the answer is “tired” children.
  2. Rewrite this sentence in the active voice:
    The trophy was won by our school.
    Answer: Our school won the trophy.
    In the passive voice, the subject receives the action (“the trophy was won”). In the active voice, the subject performs the action (“our school won”). Active voice is usually more direct and powerful.
  3. Choose the correct word: “I _______ been waiting for an hour.”
    A) have    B) of
    Answer: A) have
    The correct form is “I have been waiting” — “have” is an auxiliary (helping) verb that forms the present perfect tense. “Of” is a preposition, not a verb. The error “could of / should of / would of” comes from mishearing “could’ve / should’ve / would’ve” (contractions of “could have” etc.).
  4. What does the word vivid mean? Use it in a sentence of your own.
    Definition: producing strong, clear images in the mind; intensely bright, detailed, or lifelike.
    Example: “She had such a vivid memory of that afternoon that she could almost smell the sea.”
    Vivid is an adjective. Related words: vividly (adverb), vividness (noun).
  5. What is alliteration? Write your own example.
    Definition: Alliteration is the repetition of the same consonant sound at the beginning of nearby words, usually in the same phrase or sentence.
    Example: “The silver snake slithered silently through the shadows.”
    Why writers use it: to create rhythm, emphasise key words, and make a phrase memorable or musical.
Part 2 — Comprehension

Read this passage carefully, then answer the questions below:

The robot arrived on a Tuesday. It was delivered in a plain brown box, and Maya’s mother had to sign for it before the courier even looked up from her screen. Maya watched from the top of the stairs as her mother wrestled it through the front door. It was about the height of a ten-year-old, with a smooth white casing and two circular eyes that glowed faintly green. For three days it stood in the corner of the kitchen, switched off. On the fourth day, Maya plugged it in. The green eyes blinked. Then it said, in a voice that was almost but not quite human: “Hello, Maya. I’ve been waiting.”
  1. How was the robot delivered, and what did Maya’s mother have to do before it came inside?
    Answer: The robot was delivered in a plain brown box. Maya’s mother had to sign for it before the courier even looked up from her screen.
  2. How does the writer describe the robot’s height?
    Answer: It was described as “about the height of a ten-year-old.” This comparison helps the reader picture the robot’s size immediately, without needing measurements.
  3. Why is the phrase “almost but not quite human” an effective description of the robot’s voice?
    Example answer: The phrase is effective because it creates an unsettling, uncanny feeling. Something that is almost human is more disturbing than something that is clearly mechanical — it sits in an uncomfortable in-between space. The reader can almost hear a human voice, but knows it isn’t one, which makes the robot feel eerie and slightly threatening rather than simply harmless.
  4. What is surprising about the robot’s first words? Explain the effect on the reader.
    Example answer: The robot says “I’ve been waiting” — which implies it was aware and conscious even while switched off for three days. This is deeply surprising because a machine should not be able to “wait” in any meaningful sense. It suggests the robot has inner experience, creating a sense of unease and mystery. The reader is left wondering: what else does it know?
  5. How does the writer create mystery before the robot speaks? Give two examples from the text.
    Example answer:
    1. The robot stands “switched off” for three days — the delay builds tension and makes the reader curious about when and how it will finally be activated.
    2. The detail that the courier “didn’t look up from her screen” suggests the delivery is treated as mundane, which contrasts with how extraordinary the robot turns out to be. The ordinary surroundings make the robot’s strangeness more striking.
Part 3 — Writing Task
Prompt: The robot follows Maya to school the next morning. Write the scene: describe the playground falling silent as they arrive, contrast the reactions of different children (dialogue, fear, curiosity), and include Maya’s inner thoughts as she feels a knot in her stomach.

Write 8–10 sentences. Try to use:
  • Dialogue that shows character — what does Finn say as he steps back?
  • A contrast between the older children’s fear and the younger children’s curiosity
  • At least one sentence of Maya’s inner thought in italics
Example answer:
The playground fell silent the moment they walked through the gate. Even the Year Threes stopped their chasing game and stood with open mouths, watching the smooth white figure walk calmly at Maya’s side. Finn stepped back, his bag clutched to his chest. “What is that?” he said, as if the robot couldn’t hear him. “That,” said Maya, trying to keep her voice steady, “is my robot.” The younger children were braver — a girl with two red plaits reached out and touched the robot’s arm, then giggled. But the green eyes tracked every movement, and Maya felt the knot tighten in her stomach. What if I’ve made a terrible mistake? The robot turned its head slowly and looked at her. “Good morning, Maya,” it said. “You appear to be nervous.”

Check your own writing for:
• Contrast — older children afraid vs. younger children curious
• Italicised inner thought — shows Maya’s private worry
• Powerful verbs — “tracked,” “tighten,” “stepped back”

Bonus Maths Challenge

~5 minutes · optional
Harder Problems — Push Yourself!

These questions extend today’s topics. Show all working.

  1. What is the HCF of 48 and 60?
    Answer: 12
    Factors of 48: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 48.
    Factors of 60: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60.
    The highest factor on both lists is 12.
  2. A map has a scale of 1:25,000. Two villages are 12 cm apart on the map. What is the real distance in kilometres?
    Answer: 3 km
    Real distance = 12 × 25,000 = 300,000 cm.
    Convert: 300,000 cm ÷ 100 = 3,000 m ÷ 1,000 = 3 km.
  3. The mean of 6 numbers is 15. Five of the numbers are 12, 18, 14, 17, and 15. What is the sixth number?
    Answer: 14
    Total of all 6 numbers = mean × count = 15 × 6 = 90.
    Sum of the five known numbers = 12 + 18 + 14 + 17 + 15 = 76.
    Sixth number = 90 − 76 = 14.

Word of the Day

~2 minutes
uncanny adjective
un-KAN-ee
Definition: strangely familiar yet unsettling; beyond what is natural or expected, in a way that feels eerie.
“The robot’s smile was uncanny — almost warm, almost kind, but with something behind its green eyes that Maya could not name.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using uncanny about a moment when something familiar suddenly felt strange — perhaps a reflection, a dream, or a place you knew well.

Tip: Uncanny is perfect for describing the robot passage — using it in a comprehension answer shows you understand how the writer creates unease. For example: “The writer creates an uncanny effect by making the robot’s voice almost human, but not quite.”
