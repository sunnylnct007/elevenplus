📐 Maths (~20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. Solve: 2(3x + 1) = 5x + 9.
    x = 7. Expand: 6x + 2 = 5x + 9. Subtract 5x: x + 2 = 9. x = 7.
  2. What is 3/8 + 5/6?
    29/24 = 1 5/24. LCM of 8 and 6 = 24. 3/8 = 9/24, 5/6 = 20/24. Sum = 29/24.
  3. A number is 6 less than three times another number. If the smaller number is 9, what is the larger?
    21. Larger = (3 × 9) − 6 = 27 − 6 = 21.
  4. What is the interior angle of a regular hexagon?
    120°. Sum of interior angles = (6−2) × 180° = 720°. Each angle = 720° ÷ 6 = 120°.
  5. Estimate 49.7 × 19.8 by rounding to 1 significant figure, then find the exact answer.
    Estimate: 50 × 20 = 1,000. Exact: 49.7 × 20 − 49.7 × 0.2 = 994 − 9.94 = 984.06.
Pythagoras — Ladder against a wall

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. In an election, candidate A gets 48%, candidate B 35%, candidate C the rest — out of 2,500 votes. How many votes does each candidate get?
    A: 1,200. B: 875. C: 17% × 2500 = 425. Check: 1200+875+425 = 2,500. ✓
  2. A 1.8 m ladder leans against a wall. Its base is 0.6 m from the wall. How high up the wall does it reach? (Give to 1 d.p.)
    1.7 m. h² = 1.8² − 0.6² = 3.24 − 0.36 = 2.88. h = √2.88 ≈ 1.697 ≈ 1.7 m.
  3. A shop has a "buy 2 get 1 free" offer on bottles of juice at £1.40 each. Cost for 9 bottles?
    £8.40. Every 3 bottles costs 2 × £1.40 = £2.80. 9 bottles = 3 groups: 3 × £2.80 = £8.40.
  4. Sequence: 2, 5, 10, 17, 26, … What is the pattern? What is the next term?
    Differences: 3, 5, 7, 9 (increasing odd numbers). Next difference = 11. Next term = 26 + 11 = 37. Pattern: n² + 1.
  5. A rectangular pool (50 m × 20 m) is surrounded by a path 2 m wide. What is the total area of the path?
    296 m². Outer: (50+4) × (20+4) = 54 × 24 = 1,296 m². Inner pool: 50 × 20 = 1,000 m². Path = 296 m².

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is a non-finite clause? Give two examples.
    A clause with a verb not inflected for tense — using an infinitive or -ing/-ed form without a subject. Examples: "To win the race, she trained every day." / "Exhausted by the climb, he sat down." These are sophisticated sentence openers.
  2. What is the difference between denotation and connotation? Illustrate with the word snake.
    Denotation = literal dictionary meaning. Snake: a limbless reptile. Connotation = associations the word carries. Snake: deception, danger, betrayal. Writers choose words for their connotations as much as their denotations.
  3. Identify the grammatical error and correct it: Each of the students have submitted their essay on time.
    "have" → "has." "Each" is singular, so the verb must be singular: "Each of the students has submitted their essay on time."
  4. What does ubiquitous mean? Use it in a sentence about modern technology.
    Seeming to appear everywhere at the same time; omnipresent. Example: "Smartphones have become so ubiquitous that it is now unusual to see a person without one."
  5. What is zoomorphism? How does it differ from personification?
    Zoomorphism applies animal characteristics to humans or objects: "He prowled the room like a predator." Personification is the reverse — applying human characteristics to non-human things: "The wind howled." Personification humanises; zoomorphism animalises.

2. Comprehension — The Night Market

The night market exists only in the hours between midnight and dawn, in a city that would rather not know about it. You will not find it on any map. Word of mouth brings you there — a whispered address, a described alleyway, a particular door. Inside, traders sell things that are difficult to categorise: regrets, bottled in blue glass; memories of summer, wrapped in yellow paper; the precise feeling of being seven years old on a Saturday morning, sold for the price of a single coin. Nobody haggles. Nobody argues. Buyers leave quietly, clutching their parcels with both hands, careful not to let go. Whether the contents are real is a question the market does not encourage you to ask.
  1. What does "a city that would rather not know about it" suggest?
    It suggests the market exists in a morally ambiguous space — the city is aware but chooses to ignore it. This anthropomorphises the city, giving it a complicit attitude: willful ignorance rather than genuine unawareness.
  2. What kinds of things are sold at the market? What do they have in common?
    Regrets, memories, and childhood feelings — all intangible emotional experiences. What they share: they are abstract and deeply personal, normally impossible to possess or trade.
  3. Analyse the image "regrets, bottled in blue glass." What makes it effective?
    It makes an abstract emotion (regret) tangible — it has a colour (blue, suggesting sadness) and a container (glass, suggesting fragility). Bottling implies regrets can be contained and perhaps set aside. The specificity makes the writing vivid rather than vaguely fantastical.
  4. What is the effect of the final sentence?
    "Does not encourage" is euphemistic and slightly ominous — suggesting not just discouragement but a warning. It leaves the reader uncertain, like the buyers: unwilling to look too closely. It also asks: what does "real" mean for an emotion or a memory?
  5. How does the writer create a dreamlike atmosphere?
    Through: nocturnal setting; deliberate vagueness about location; impossible goods; the quiet, ritualistic behaviour of buyers; direct address "you." The calm, matter-of-fact prose makes the fantastical feel more real — refusal to marvel at what it describes increases the uncanny effect.

3. Writing task

You visit the night market and choose one item to buy. Describe what it is, what it looks like, and what happens when you open it at home.

Use: matter-of-fact tone (describe impossible things calmly), specific sensory details, and an ambiguous ending.

I chose a small jar, no bigger than my fist, sealed with black wax and labelled in handwriting I almost recognised. The trader said nothing. I paid without counting the coins. At home, I sat across from it for a long time. When I finally broke the seal, there was no smell, no smoke — just a faint warmth that spread up from my palms. And then I was eleven years old, standing in my grandmother's garden on a summer evening, the grass cool beneath my bare feet, her voice calling from inside the house. It lasted perhaps four seconds. When I came back to the kitchen, the jar was empty. I put the lid back on anyway. I didn't throw it away.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Pythagoras: A right-angled triangle has legs of 9 cm and 12 cm. Find its hypotenuse and area.
    Hypotenuse = 15 cm. (9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225; √225 = 15.) Area = ½ × 9 × 12 = 54 cm².
  2. Fractions & algebra: Solve: 3/8 + x/4 = 1.
    x = 5/2 = 2.5. x/4 = 1 − 3/8 = 5/8. x = 5/8 × 4 = 5/2.
  3. Compound sequences: A sequence is: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, … (each term = sum of previous two). What is the 10th term?
    55. Continue: 21, 34, 55. (This is the Fibonacci sequence.)

💬 Word of the Day

liminal
adjective
Relating to a threshold or transitional stage between two states or worlds. From Latin limen — threshold.
"The night market occupied a liminal space — neither fully real nor entirely imagined."
