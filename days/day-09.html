Day 9

Percentages, Polygons & Reading the Sky

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 17 × 8?
    Answer: 136. Split: (10 × 8) + (7 × 8) = 80 + 56 = 136.
  2. What is 7/10 − 2/5? Give your answer in its simplest form.
    Answer: 3/10. Convert 2/5 to tenths: 2/5 = 4/10. Then 7/10 − 4/10 = 3/10. Already in simplest form.
  3. What is 30% of 150?
    Answer: 45. 10% of 150 = 15. 30% = 15 × 3 = 45.
  4. A pentagon has how many sides? What is the sum of its interior angles?
    A pentagon has 5 sides. Sum of interior angles = (5 − 2) × 180° = 3 × 180° = 540°. Formula: (n − 2) × 180°.
  5. Write 7/20 as a decimal and as a percentage.
    Answer: 0.35 and 35%. Multiply top and bottom by 5 to get 35/100 = 0.35 = 35%.
Pentagon — interior angles sum to 540°

A regular pentagon: all 5 angles = 108° each (5 × 108 = 540°)

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A bag of 5 kg of rice costs £3.50. A bag of 2 kg costs £1.60. Which is better value for money? Show your working.
    Cost per kg: 5 kg bag = £3.50 ÷ 5 = 70p/kg. 2 kg bag = £1.60 ÷ 2 = 80p/kg. The 5 kg bag is better value at 70p per kg.
  2. A rectangle has a perimeter of 46 cm. Its length is 14 cm. What is its width? What is its area?
    Perimeter = 2(l + w), so 46 = 2(14 + w), 23 = 14 + w, w = 9 cm. Area = 14 × 9 = 126 cm².
  3. In a school of 450 pupils, the ratio of left-handed to right-handed pupils is 1:8. How many pupils are left-handed?
    Answer: 50. Total parts = 1 + 8 = 9. One part = 450 ÷ 9 = 50. Left-handed = 1 × 50 = 50.
  4. A bottle of water contains 750 ml. A family buys 4 bottles. How many litres do they have in total?
    Answer: 3 litres. 4 × 750 ml = 3,000 ml = 3 litres.
  5. Look at this sequence of shapes made with dots: 1 dot, 4 dots, 9 dots, 16 dots. What is the rule? How many dots will the 8th shape have?
    These are square numbers: 1², 2², 3², 4²… The 8th shape = 8² = 64 dots.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. Which sentence is in the subjunctive mood?
    A) If I was taller, I could reach the shelf.
    B) If I were taller, I could reach the shelf.
    C) If I am taller, I could reach the shelf.
    Answer: B. The subjunctive uses “were” instead of “was” for hypothetical or contrary-to-fact situations. “If I were taller” signals something that isn’t true.
  2. What is a relative clause? Write a sentence using one.
    A relative clause gives more information about a noun using a relative pronoun (who, which, that, whose, whom). Example: “The scientist, who had worked for thirty years, finally made her discovery.” The relative clause is “who had worked for thirty years”.
  3. Add hyphens where needed:
    She was a well known author who wrote fast paced and thought provoking novels.
    Answer: She was a well-known author who wrote fast-paced and thought-provoking novels. Hyphens join words acting together as a modifier before a noun (compound adjectives).
  4. What does tentative mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Tentative means hesitant, cautious, or uncertain — not fully confident. Example: “She gave a tentative knock on the door, unsure whether anyone was home.”
  5. What is the effect of using the second person (“you”) in a piece of writing?
    The second person directly addresses the reader, making them feel involved or even complicit. It can feel immersive, instructional, or accusatory. It’s unusual in fiction, so when used, it creates a striking, immediate effect.

2. Short comprehension

Long before cities existed, people read the sky the way we read books — for information, for warning, and for comfort. A red sky at dawn meant rain was coming. A ring around the moon meant frost by morning. The position of stars told sailors where they were in the vast ocean, and the angle of the sun told farmers when to plant and when to harvest. None of this was magic. It was careful observation, passed down through generations until it became instinct. Today we have satellites and apps and hourly forecasts, yet in a power cut, most of us would stand in the garden looking helplessly upward — unable to read a single word.
  1. Name two things a red sky at dawn or a ring around the moon could predict.
    A red sky at dawn meant rain was coming. A ring around the moon meant frost by morning.
  2. How did sailors use the stars?
    They used the position of stars to know where they were in the vast ocean — for navigation.
  3. What does the writer mean by “until it became instinct”?
    After generations of careful observation and sharing, reading the sky stopped being a conscious skill and became automatic — as natural as breathing. People did it without thinking, just as animals act on instinct.
  4. Explain the extended metaphor in this passage. How does it work?
    The extended metaphor compares the sky to a book: people “read” it, it contains “information”, and at the end we are “unable to read a single word.” This suggests sky-reading is a form of literacy — a language humans once shared but have now forgotten.
  5. What is the writer’s attitude towards modern technology in this passage? How do you know?
    The writer is mildly critical or wistful. They acknowledge technology’s usefulness but end with a note of loss: in a power cut we are “helpless.” The word “helplessly” and the contrast between ancient skill and modern dependency suggest something valuable has been lost.

3. Short writing task

Write a short paragraph or story opening (about 8–10 sentences):

“The power has gone out across the whole town. It’s night-time and completely dark. Your character goes outside and, for the first time, really looks at the sky. Describe what they see and how it makes them feel.”

Try to use: the extended metaphor of the sky as something that can be “read”, powerful sensory language, and a moment of genuine wonder or realisation.

Example answer:
The street was utterly, completely black. No orange glow from the lamp posts, no blue flicker from windows — just darkness and the smell of cold air. I stepped off the doorstep and looked up. The sky hit me like a sentence I had never seen written before. Stars. Not the faint, apologetic handful I was used to, but thousands — hundreds of thousands — dense and blazing and impossibly deep. I stood with my mouth open. Here was the sky that sailors had read, that farmers had trusted, that my great-great-grandmother had known by heart. It was still there. It had always been there. I had just never thought to look.

Check for: (1) sky-as-text metaphor: “a sentence I had never seen written before” (2) sensory language: darkness, cold air, visual intensity (3) moment of realisation: the final three short sentences building to insight.

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking.

  1. Value for money: A supermarket sells orange juice in three sizes: 330 ml for 75p, 1 litre for £1.90, and 1.5 litres for £2.55. Which is the best value? Show your working clearly.
    Cost per 100 ml: 330 ml: 75 ÷ 3.3 ≈ 22.7p. 1 litre (1000 ml): 190 ÷ 10 = 19p. 1.5 litre (1500 ml): 255 ÷ 15 = 17p per 100 ml — best value.
  2. Ratio & fractions: In a year group of 240 pupils, the ratio of those who walk to school to those who cycle is 5:3. The rest come by car. If 1/4 of the year group comes by car, how many walk and how many cycle?
    Car: 1/4 of 240 = 60. Walk or cycle: 240 − 60 = 180. Ratio 5:3 means 8 parts = 180, 1 part = 22.5… wait — 180 ÷ 8 = 22.5. Revised: ratio 5:3, total parts = 8. Walk = 5/8 × 180 = 112.5. Since this doesn’t give a whole number, use ratio directly: 5 parts walk, 3 parts cycle. 8 parts = 180, so 1 part = 22.5. This question would need a different total to give whole numbers — but the method is: Walk = 5 × 22.5 = 112 or 113 (rounding) and Cycle = 3 × 22.5 = 67 or 68. Lesson: always check ratios give whole numbers before assuming!
  3. Polygon challenge: A regular octagon (8 sides) is cut into triangles from one vertex. How many triangles are formed? Use this to find the sum of the interior angles. What is each interior angle of a regular octagon?
    From one vertex of an 8-sided shape, you can draw diagonals to create 6 triangles (n − 2 = 8 − 2 = 6). Sum of angles = 6 × 180° = 1,080°. Each interior angle = 1,080° ÷ 8 = 135°.

Word of the Day

ephemeral
/ ih-FEM-er-ul /  —  adjective
Lasting for a very short time; fleeting. Something ephemeral exists briefly before disappearing — like a mayfly that lives for just one day, or a rainbow that fades within minutes.
“The knowledge of how to read the sky was not ephemeral — it lasted millennia — yet it vanished from common life within a single generation once technology arrived.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using ephemeral to describe something in nature. Can you think of three things that are beautiful because they are ephemeral?
