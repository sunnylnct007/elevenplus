Maths

~20 minutes
Part 1 — Core Skills Warm-up

Answer these quickly — mentally or with brief working:

  1. What is 6 × 14?
    Answer: 84
    Split it: (6 × 10) + (6 × 4) = 60 + 24 = 84.
  2. What is the lowest common multiple (LCM) of 4 and 6?
    Answer: 12
    Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16…
    Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18…
    The first number on both lists is 12.
  3. Write these fractions in order from smallest to largest: ½, ⅖, ¾, 3/10.
    Answer: 3/10, ⅖, ½, ¾
    Convert to 20ths: 3/10 = 6/20  •  2/5 = 8/20  •  1/2 = 10/20  •  3/4 = 15/20.
    Order: 6/20 < 8/20 < 10/20 < 15/20.
    0 1
    3/10
    2/5
    1/2
    3/4
  4. What is 8² (eight squared)?
    Answer: 64
    8² means 8 × 8 = 64. The small raised 2 means “multiplied by itself twice.”
  5. A triangle has angles of 90°, 55°, and 35°. What type of triangle is it? What do the angles add up to?
    Type: right-angled triangle (one angle is exactly 90°).
    Sum: 180° — 90 + 55 + 35 = 180. All triangles have angles that sum to exactly 180°.
Part 2 — Word Problems & Exam-Style Practice

Show clear working for each question:

  1. A car travels at a steady speed of 60 km/h. How far does it travel in 2 hours and 30 minutes?
    Answer: 150 km
    2 hours: 60 × 2 = 120 km.
    30 min (half an hour): 60 ÷ 2 = 30 km.
    Total: 120 + 30 = 150 km.
  2. In a survey of 200 pupils, ⅜ said their favourite subject was Maths. How many pupils did NOT choose Maths?
    Answer: 125 pupils
    ⅛ of 200 = 25.  ⅜ = 3 × 25 = 75 chose Maths.
    200 − 75 = 125 did not choose Maths.
  3. A cuboid has length 5 cm, width 4 cm, and height 3 cm. What is its volume?
    Answer: 60 cm³
    Volume = length × width × height = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 cm³.
    V = l × w × h
    = 5 × 4 × 3
    = 60 cm³
  4. Zara scores 18 out of 25 in a test. What percentage did she score?
    Answer: 72%
    Method 1: × top and bottom by 4 → 18/25 = 72/100 = 72%.
    Method 2: 18 ÷ 25 = 0.72, then × 100 = 72%.
  5. A rectangle has an area of 84 cm². Its width is 6 cm. What is its length? What is its perimeter?
    Length = 14 cm — Area ÷ width = 84 ÷ 6 = 14 cm.
    Perimeter = 40 cm — 2 × (14 + 6) = 2 × 20 = 40 cm.

English

~20–25 minutes
Part 1 — SPaG & Vocabulary
  1. Which sentence uses the apostrophe correctly?
    A) The dog’s bone was buried in the garden.
    B) The dogs’ bone was buried in the garden.
    C) Both A and B are correct, depending on the meaning.
    Answer: C
    “The dog’s bone” = bone belonging to one dog (apostrophe before the s).
    “The dogs’ bone” = bone belonging to more than one dog (apostrophe after the s).
    Both are correct — the position of the apostrophe tells us how many owners there are.
  2. Add the correct suffix to turn each word into an adverb:
    (i) brave    (ii) happy    (iii) gentle
    (i) bravely — add -ly directly.
    (ii) happily — change y to i, then add -ly (happy → happily).
    (iii) gently — words ending consonant + le drop the e before -ly (gentle → gently).
  3. Identify the subordinate clause in this sentence:
    Although she was nervous, Clara walked onto the stage.
    Subordinate clause: “Although she was nervous”
    It cannot stand alone as a sentence — it depends on the main clause (“Clara walked onto the stage”) to make sense. It begins with a subordinating conjunction: although.
  4. What is the difference between a metaphor and a simile? Give one example of each.
    Simile — compares using “like” or “as”: “Her eyes were like stars.”
    Metaphor — says something IS something else: “Her eyes were stars.”
    A metaphor is often considered stronger because it makes a bold, direct claim rather than a comparison.
  5. Choose the correct word to complete the sentence:
    The explorers walked along the rocky _______ of the cliff.
    A) led    B) ledge    C) edge    D) lease
    Answer: C) edge (or B) ledge — both fit grammatically and in meaning).
    “Edge” = the rim or border. “Ledge” = a narrow flat shelf on a cliff. “Led” is the past tense of lead. “Lease” = a rental agreement — doesn’t fit at all!
Part 2 — Comprehension

Read this passage carefully, then answer the questions:

Nobody had expected the storm. One moment the sky was a wide, pale blue; the next, it turned the colour of a bruise. Lightning tore across the horizon in jagged white lines, and the thunder that followed shook the windows of the little lighthouse. Keeper Aldous stood at the top of his tower, watching. Below, the sea had become a furious animal — heaving, crashing, flinging white foam against the rocks. He had kept this lighthouse for thirty years. He had seen storms like this before. But something was different tonight: in the distance, through the driving rain, he could make out the small, dark shape of a boat.
  1. How did the sky change at the start of the storm?
    Answer: It changed from “wide, pale blue” to “the colour of a bruise” — turning dark purple/grey very suddenly. The writer uses “one moment… the next” to show how fast this happened.
  2. What is Aldous’s job, and how long has he been doing it?
    Answer: He is a lighthouse keeper and has kept this lighthouse for thirty years.
  3. “The sea had become a furious animal.” What effect does this metaphor create?
    Example answer: The metaphor makes the sea seem wild, unpredictable, and dangerous — as though it has its own rage and power. The verbs that follow (“heaving, crashing, flinging”) build on this by giving the sea violent, animal-like actions. It makes the storm feel threatening and alive rather than just a weather event.
  4. How does the writer build tension at the end of the passage? Comment on both structure and language.
    Example answer: Two short sentences (“He had kept this lighthouse for thirty years. He had seen storms like this before.”) suggest Aldous is experienced and calm — then the word “But” signals a change. The final long sentence delays the key detail (the boat) until the very end, building suspense. The phrase “through the driving rain” adds difficulty and drama just before the revelation.
  5. What might happen next? Use evidence from the passage to support your prediction.
    Example answer: Aldous may attempt a rescue — he is an experienced keeper (“thirty years”) who notices the boat immediately despite the storm. The word “different” hints that something unusual is about to happen, and the boat in danger suggests a rescue mission is coming.
Part 3 — Writing Task
Prompt: Aldous climbs back down from the lighthouse tower and prepares to go out in the storm. Describe his thoughts and actions as he gets ready. What does he take with him? How does he feel?

Write 8–10 sentences. Try to use:
  • A mixture of short punchy sentences and longer descriptive ones
  • Powerful verbs (not just “walked” or “picked up”)
  • At least one piece of direct thought shown in italics or speech marks
Example answer:
Aldous descended the spiral staircase two steps at a time, his hand barely touching the cold iron rail. His mind was already at the water’s edge. He hauled on his oilskin jacket and jammed his boots onto his feet without unlacing them. The rope. He would need the rope. He coiled it over his shoulder, then grabbed the lantern from its hook by the door. “Thirty years,” he muttered to himself, “and I’ve never lost a soul yet.” He wasn’t about to start tonight. The door screamed on its hinges as the wind wrenched it open, and the rain hit him like a wall of cold needles. He stepped out into the dark.

Check your own writing for:
• Powerful verbs — “descended, hauled, jammed, coiled, wrenched” rather than “went, put on, took”
• Sentence variety — short sentences (“The rope. He would need the rope.”) for urgency
• Direct thought or speech — we hear Aldous’s voice, making him feel real

Bonus Maths Challenge

~5 minutes · optional
Harder Problems — Push Yourself!

These questions extend today’s topics. Show all working.

  1. A boat travels 45 km at 15 km/h, then a further 60 km at 20 km/h. What is the total journey time in hours and minutes?
    Answer: 6 hours
    Leg 1: 45 ÷ 15 = 3 hours.
    Leg 2: 60 ÷ 20 = 3 hours.
    Total = 3 + 3 = 6 hours.
  2. A swimming pool is a cuboid 25 m long, 10 m wide, and 2 m deep. Water is pumped in at 5,000 litres per minute. How many minutes does it take to fill? (1 m³ = 1,000 litres)
    Answer: 100 minutes
    Volume = 25 × 10 × 2 = 500 m³.
    In litres: 500 × 1,000 = 500,000 litres.
    Time = 500,000 ÷ 5,000 = 100 minutes.
  3. Which is larger: &frac57; or &frac79;? Show how you know.
    Answer: &frac79; is larger.
    Common denominator of 7 and 9 is 63.
    5/7 = 45/63  •  7/9 = 49/63.
    49/63 > 45/63, so 7/9 > 5/7.

Word of the Day

~2 minutes
indomitable adjective
in-DOM-it-uh-bul
Definition: impossible to subdue or defeat; having a will or spirit that refuses to give in, no matter the difficulty.
“Despite thirty years of storms and sleepless nights, Keeper Aldous remained indomitable — always the first to answer the sea’s call for help.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using indomitable about a person — real or fictional — who overcomes a challenge. It could be a scientist, an athlete, a character from a book, or someone you admire.

Tip: Indomitable is a powerful word to use when writing about a character’s inner strength. In a comprehension question about how a writer presents a character, using this word in your answer shows real sophistication.
