📐 Maths (~20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. Solve simultaneously: x + y = 10 and x − y = 4.
    x = 7, y = 3. Add the equations: 2x = 14 → x = 7. Subtract to find y: 10 − 7 = 3.
  2. What is 0.4% of 5,000?
    20. 1% = 50. 0.4% = 50 × 0.4 = 20.
  3. A parallelogram has base 9 cm and height 6 cm. Find its area.
    54 cm². A = base × height = 9 × 6 = 54 cm².
  4. What is (−3)³?
    −27. (−3) × (−3) = 9. 9 × (−3) = −27.
  5. What fraction of 3 hours is 45 minutes?
    1/4. 3 hours = 180 minutes. 45/180 = 1/4.

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A 600 ml bottle of juice contains 30% real fruit. How many ml of real fruit does it contain?
    180 ml. 30% × 600 = 180 ml.
  2. A two-digit number: the digits sum to 11 and reversing the digits gives a number 27 more. What is the original number?
    47. Let tens digit = a, units = b. a+b=11 and (10b+a)−(10a+b)=27 → 9b−9a=27 → b−a=3. Solving: a=4, b=7. Number = 47.
  3. The mean of 5 numbers is 18. Four of the numbers are 14, 22, 9, 25. What is the fifth number?
    20. Total = 18 × 5 = 90. Sum of four: 14+22+9+25 = 70. Fifth number = 90 − 70 = 20.
  4. A semicircle has diameter 20 cm. Find its total perimeter (straight edge + curved edge). (π = 3.14)
    51.4 cm. Curved arc = π × r = 3.14 × 10 = 31.4 cm. Add diameter: 31.4 + 20 = 51.4 cm.
  5. Share £1,260 in ratio 2:5:7. Find each share.
    £180, £450, £630. Total parts = 14. One part = £90. Shares: 2×90=£180, 5×90=£450, 7×90=£630.

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is chiasmus? Give one famous example.
    Chiasmus reverses the structure of a phrase for effect (A-B-B-A pattern). Famous example: "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country" (JFK). The reversal creates balance and memorability.
  2. What is the difference between tone and mood in a text?
    Tone = the writer's attitude towards the subject (e.g. ironic, admiring, mournful). Mood = the atmosphere the reader experiences (e.g. tense, dreamy, melancholy). A horror story might have a fearful mood but a calm, detached tone.
  3. Correct the apostrophe errors: Its important that all of the students parents' attend. The school's rules are clear.
    It's important that all of the students' parents attend. The school's rules are clear. (Its→It's; parents', not students'; the apostrophe after students' shows possession by multiple students.)
  4. What does nonchalant mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Casually calm and relaxed; appearing not to care or worry. Example: "He gave a nonchalant shrug when told he had won, as if the outcome had never been in doubt."
  5. What is the effect of beginning a sentence with a conjunction ("But", "And", "Because")? When should this be used?
    Starting with a conjunction creates emphasis, a conversational tone, or a dramatic pause. "But she did not come." — the single word sets up expectation, then delivers a punchy statement. Used sparingly for effect; overuse loses impact. Now grammatically accepted in literary writing.

2. Comprehension — The Last Day

On the morning of her last day, my grandmother asked to be taken outside. It was October, and cold, but she was specific: she wanted to sit in the garden, in the metal chair near the apple tree, with a cup of tea she would not finish. We wrapped her in blankets and carried the chair, and she sat there for perhaps an hour while we moved around her quietly, uncertain what to do with our hands. She did not speak much. She looked at the apple tree, which still had a few fruit on it, small and forgotten. Once she said: "I planted that." She said it the way you would note a fact you were glad to know. When we brought her back inside, she slept for most of the afternoon. She died that night, as quietly as she had always done everything.
  1. What did the grandmother ask for on her last day, and what details make this specific?
    To sit outside in the garden — specifically in the metal chair near the apple tree, with a cup of tea. The specificity makes the scene vivid and the request seem purposeful.
  2. What is the effect of "a cup of tea she would not finish"?
    It signals, without stating it, that she is dying — she asks for the gesture of tea rather than the tea itself. This understatement is more affecting than a direct statement about her condition.
  3. Analyse the sentence "I planted that," including how she says it.
    She says it "the way you would note a fact you were glad to know" — with quiet satisfaction, not sentiment. Short, plain, and possessive. It is her final act of taking stock: something of hers will outlast her.
  4. How is the family's discomfort conveyed without direct description of their emotions?
    "Uncertain what to do with our hands" — physical awkwardness stands in for emotional helplessness. This concrete body-language detail conveys grief and inadequacy more powerfully than abstract emotional language.
  5. How does the final sentence mirror the grandmother's character throughout the passage?
    "As quietly as she had always done everything." — her death characterises her entire life. She was consistent to the end. The adverb "quietly" echoes her not speaking much, her plain words, and the simplicity of her request.

3. Writing task

Write a short piece about a person through a single specific memory — not a summary of who they were, but one scene that captures them. Use: precise sensory details, one thing they said or did that reveals character, and a final sentence that reaches beyond the memory.

My uncle always brought oranges. Not flowers, not wine — oranges, in a paper bag, which he would set on the table without explanation, as if it were perfectly obvious why. He peeled one every time, methodically, in one long spiral, then divided it into sections and handed them around without asking if anyone wanted one. He assumed you did. I never saw anyone refuse. I think of this now when I feel overwhelmed by other people's fussiness about what they will and will not accept. Just hand them the orange. Most of the time, they will take it.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Simultaneous equations: 2x + 3y = 12 and x − y = 1. Find x and y.
    x = 3, y = 2. From second equation: x = y + 1. Substitute: 2(y+1) + 3y = 12 → 5y + 2 = 12 → y = 2. x = 3.
  2. Ratio & digits: A three-digit number has digits in ratio 1:2:3 (hundreds:tens:units). The number is divisible by 9. What is it?
    123. Digits: 1, 2, 3 (ratio 1:2:3). Sum = 1+2+3 = 6 — not divisible by 9. Try scaling: 2, 4, 6 → sum = 12, not divisible by 9. Try 3, 6, 9 → sum = 18, divisible by 9. Answer: 369.
  3. Mean & algebra: The mean of n numbers is 14. When a value of 26 is added, the mean rises to 15. Find n.
    n = 11. Original sum = 14n. New sum = 14n + 26. New mean: (14n + 26)/(n+1) = 15. So 14n + 26 = 15n + 15. n = 11.

💬 Word of the Day

legacy
noun
Something handed down from a predecessor; a lasting impact left by a person's actions or achievements. From Latin legatus — one who is sent as a representative.
"The apple tree in the garden was her most enduring legacy — outlasting memory, still bearing fruit."
