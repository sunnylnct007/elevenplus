Maths

~20 minutes
Part 1 — Core Skills Warm-up

Answer these quickly — mentally or with brief working:

  1. What is 8 × 12?
    Answer: 96
    Split it: (8 × 10) + (8 × 2) = 80 + 16 = 96.
  2. What is 132 ÷ 11?
    Answer: 12
    Check: 11 × 10 = 110, then 11 × 2 = 22, so 110 + 22 = 132. ✓
  3. Write ¾ as a decimal and as a percentage.
    Answer: 0.75 and 75%
    3 ÷ 4 = 0.75. Then 0.75 × 100 = 75%.
  4. What is the value of the digit 6 in the number 46,372?
    Answer: 6,000 (six thousand)
    The digits in 46,372 are: 4 = ten-thousands, 6 = thousands, 3 = hundreds, 7 = tens, 2 = units.
  5. What is 15% of 60?
    Answer: 9
    10% of 60 = 6.  5% = half of that = 3.  15% = 6 + 3 = 9.
Part 2 — Word Problems & Exam-Style Practice

Show clear working for each question:

  1. A train timetable shows these departure times from London: 07:15, 08:05, 09:50, 11:30. What is the gap in minutes between the second and third departure?
    Answer: 105 minutes
    08:05 → 09:05 = 60 min.  09:05 → 09:50 = 45 min.  Total = 60 + 45 = 105 min.
  2. A box of 24 pencils costs £3.60. What is the cost of one pencil?
    Answer: 15p
    £3.60 ÷ 24 = £0.15 = 15p.
  3. A rectangle has a length of 14 cm and a width of 7 cm. What is its perimeter? What is its area?
    Perimeter = 42 cm
    P = 2 × (14 + 7) = 2 × 21 = 42 cm.

    Area = 98 cm²
    A = 14 × 7 = 98 cm².
  4. In a class of 32 pupils, ⅜ walk to school. How many pupils do NOT walk to school?
    Answer: 20 pupils
    ⅛ of 32 = 32 ÷ 8 = 4.  So ⅜ = 3 × 4 = 12 walk.  32 − 12 = 20 do not walk.
  5. A sequence starts: 4, 11, 18, 25, … What are the next two terms? What is the rule?
    Next two terms: 32 and 39
    Rule: add 7 each time.  25 + 7 = 32,  32 + 7 = 39.

English

~20–25 minutes
Part 1 — SPaG & Vocabulary
  1. Which sentence uses a comma correctly?
    A) Although it was raining, the match continued.
    B) Although, it was raining the match continued.
    C) Although it was raining the, match continued.
    Answer: A
    When a sentence begins with a subordinate clause (“Although it was raining”), a comma separates it from the main clause. The comma goes after the subordinate clause, not inside it.
  2. Rewrite this sentence in the past tense:
    The children run across the field and climb the oak tree.
    Answer: The children ran across the field and climbed the oak tree.
    “Run” → “ran” (irregular); “climb” → “climbed” (regular, add -ed).
  3. Identify the adverb in this sentence:
    She quietly opened the door and slipped outside.
    Answer: quietly
    An adverb modifies a verb — here it tells us how she opened the door. (“Outside” is also an adverb of place, so either is acceptable.)
  4. Choose the correct word to complete the sentence:
    The team had to decide _______ to continue the game.
    A) weather    B) whether    C) wether
    Answer: B) whether
    “Whether” introduces alternatives or choices. “Weather” = rain/sun etc. “Wether” = a castrated sheep!
  5. What is a metaphor? Write your own example.
    A metaphor describes something as if it IS something else, without using “like” or “as”.
    Examples: “The classroom was a zoo.”  •  “Her voice was velvet.”  •  “Time is a thief.”
Part 2 — Comprehension

Read this passage carefully, then answer the questions:

At the edge of the forest, Tomás stopped walking. He had been here a hundred times before, but tonight the trees seemed different — taller, closer together, whispering amongst themselves as the wind moved through their highest branches. The path ahead was swallowed by darkness. He pulled his torch from his pocket and clicked it on. A thin beam of yellow light cut through the black. Behind him, the village lights glittered like scattered coins. He took one deep breath, gripped his torch more tightly, and stepped forwards.
  1. Where is Tomás at the start of the passage?
    Answer: At the edge of a forest.
    The passage opens: “At the edge of the forest, Tomás stopped walking.”
  2. Has Tomás been to this place before? How do you know?
    Answer: Yes.
    “He had been here a hundred times before.” This familiarity makes his fear more striking — the place has changed, not him.
  3. Find and copy a simile from the passage.
    Answer: “like scattered coins”
    Full sentence: “the village lights glittered like scattered coins.” A simile compares using “like” or “as”.
  4. How does the writer make the forest feel threatening? Give two examples from the passage.
    Any two of these:
    • “taller, closer together” — the trees feel oppressive, closing in.
    • “whispering amongst themselves” — personification, as if the trees are conspiring.
    • “The path ahead was swallowed by darkness” — metaphor making darkness seem like a predator.
    • He “gripped his torch more tightly” — his physical action reveals his fear.
  5. What does the word swallowed suggest about the darkness?
    Example answer: “Swallowed” is a metaphor suggesting the darkness is alive and hungry — as if it has consumed or eaten the path. It makes the darkness feel powerful and dangerous, rather than simply the absence of light.
Part 3 — Writing Task
Prompt: Tomás steps into the forest. Describe what he sees, hears and smells. Something unexpected happens. What is it, and how does he react?

Write 8–10 sentences. Try to use:
  • Full stops and commas correctly
  • At least one metaphor and one simile
  • Varied sentence lengths — some short for impact, some longer for description
Example answer:
The darkness closed around Tomás like a fist. His torch threw a pale circle of light onto the muddy path, and beyond its edge the shadows stood as solid as walls. The air smelled of damp earth and something sweeter — pine resin, perhaps, or rotting leaves. An owl called once, and then fell silent. Tomás walked slowly, placing each foot with care. Then he heard it: a soft, rhythmic crunching in the undergrowth, ten metres to his left. He froze. The sound stopped. His heart was a drum beating behind his ribs as he swung his torch towards the noise and held his breath.

Check your own writing for:
• Varied sentence lengths — notice the short punch of “He froze.”
• Simile: “like a fist” / “as solid as walls”
• Metaphor: “His heart was a drum”
• Sensory detail — sight, sound and smell all used

Bonus Maths Challenge

~5 minutes · optional
Harder Problems — Push Yourself!

These questions build on today’s topics but with an extra twist. Show all working.

  1. A sequence has the rule “multiply by 3 then subtract 1”. The first term is 2. Write down the first five terms of the sequence.
    Answer: 2, 5, 14, 41, 122
    Term 1 = 2
    Term 2 = (2 × 3) − 1 = 5
    Term 3 = (5 × 3) − 1 = 14
    Term 4 = (14 × 3) − 1 = 41
    Term 5 = (41 × 3) − 1 = 122
  2. A square and a rectangle have the same perimeter of 48 cm. The rectangle’s length is 15 cm. What is the area of the square?
    Answer: 144 cm²
    Square side = 48 ÷ 4 = 12 cm.  Area = 12 × 12 = 144 cm².
    (The rectangle is a red herring — you only need the perimeter!)
  3. A shopkeeper sells a jacket for £54. This is 20% more than he paid for it. How much did he pay?
    Answer: £45
    £54 = 120% of the cost price.
    1% = £54 ÷ 120 = £0.45
    100% = £0.45 × 100 = £45.

Word of the Day

~2 minutes
foreboding noun / adjective
for-BOH-ding
Definition: a strong feeling that something bad or unpleasant is going to happen; a sense of approaching danger or doom.
“As Tomás stood at the forest’s edge, a deep foreboding crept over him — as though the trees themselves were warning him to turn back.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using foreboding set somewhere other than a forest — perhaps a school corridor, a sports ground before a match, or the moment before an exam result.

Tip: Using a word like foreboding in your writing task instantly shows the examiner you have a wide vocabulary — pair it with a short, punchy sentence for maximum effect.
