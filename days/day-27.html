Factorising, Compound Interest & Mia's Postcards

Algebra · Pythagoras · Sibilance · Empathy · Comprehension

⏱ Maths ~20 min ⏱ English ~20 min

Maths

Part 1 — Core skills warm-up
  1. Factorise: x² + 7x + 12.
    (x + 3)(x + 4). Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to 7 → 3 and 4. Check: x² + 3x + 4x + 12 = x² + 7x + 12. ✓
  2. What is 120% of £85?
    £102. 100% = £85, 20% = £17. Total = £85 + £17 = £102. Or: 85 × 1.2 = 102.
  3. A line passes through (2, 5) with gradient 3. Write its equation.
    y = 3x − 1. Using y − y₁ = m(x − x₁): y − 5 = 3(x − 2) → y = 3x − 6 + 5 → y = 3x − 1.
  4. What is the probability of NOT rolling a 6 on a fair dice?
    5/6. There are 5 outcomes that are not 6, out of 6 total. P(not 6) = 5/6.
  5. Write 7/9 as a decimal to 3 decimal places.
    0.778. 7 ÷ 9 = 0.7̄ (recurring). To 3 d.p. ≈ 0.778.

Part 2 — Word problems & exam-style practice
  1. A right-angled triangular garden has shorter sides of 8 m and 15 m. Find the hypotenuse and the area.
    Hypotenuse = 17 m; Area = 60 m². h² = 8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17². Area = ½ × 8 × 15 = 60 m².
  2. £5,000 is invested at 4% compound interest per year for 3 years. What is the final value?
    £5,624.32. 5000 × (1.04)³ = 5000 × 1.124864 = £5,624.32.
  3. 12 pupils scored A, 8 scored B, 5 scored C, 3 scored D. What percentage scored A or B?
    ≈ 71.4%. Total = 28. A or B = 20. 20 ÷ 28 × 100 ≈ 71.4%.
  4. A number divided by 7 leaves remainder 3; divided by 5 leaves remainder 2. What is the smallest such number greater than 10?
    17. 17 ÷ 7 = 2 remainder 3 ✓; 17 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 2 ✓; 17 > 10 ✓.
  5. Prove algebraically that (n + 1)² − n² = 2n + 1 for all integers n.
    (n+1)² − n²
    = n² + 2n + 1 − n²
    = 2n + 1 ✓ QED
    This proves consecutive square numbers always differ by an odd number.

English

Part 1 — SPaG & vocabulary
  1. What is sibilance? Give an example and explain its effect.
    Sibilance is the repetition of 's' and 'sh' sounds. E.g. 'The silver snake slid silently through the grass.' Can suggest stealth, mystery, or danger (hissing quality) — or softness and calm. The effect depends on context.
  2. What is the difference between sympathy and empathy in reader response?
    Sympathy = feeling sorry for a character from the outside. Empathy = feeling what the character feels, as though from the inside. Strong writing creates empathy; simply telling the reader to feel sorry produces only sympathy.
  3. What should a reliable critical response to a text include?
    Use the PEA structure: (1) Point — a clear argument. (2) Evidence — a quotation. (3) Analysis — what specific language or technique the writer uses and its effect on the reader.
  4. What does sardonic mean? How does it differ from sarcastic?
    Sardonic = grimly mocking or cynically humorous; drier and more bitter than sarcasm, often self-aware. Sarcasm is blunter. E.g. 'How refreshing,' he said sardonically, 'to be told what I already knew.'
  5. What is enjambment in poetry? Give an example.
    Enjambment is when a line break does not coincide with a grammatical pause — the sentence runs into the next line. From Keats: 'Season of mists and mellow / fruitfulness.' Creates flow and can produce suspense at the break itself.
Part 2 — Comprehension: Three Years of Her Attention
For three years, Mia sent a postcard every week to her older brother at a remote research station in northern Canada. She did not always have news. Often she wrote about very small things: a crow that had learned to use the buttons of the pedestrian crossing near her flat, a particular shade of blue she had noticed in the sky one afternoon, the fact that the café on the corner had finally changed its menu. Her brother kept every postcard. When she visited at the end of his posting, she saw them pinned to the wall above his desk, 156 of them, slightly faded, the colours of three years of her attention. 'Why did you keep them all?' she asked. 'Because,' he said, 'they proved you were still there.'
  1. What did Mia do every week, and what did she write about?
    She sent a postcard. She wrote about small ordinary things — a crow, a shade of blue, a café menu change — because she did not always have news.
  2. What does '156 postcards pinned to the wall' suggest?
    The precise large number shows accumulated devotion — small acts adding up to something significant. The fading suggests time passed; the deliberate pinning shows they were actively preserved, not forgotten.
  3. Analyse the phrase 'three years of her attention'.
    'Attention' is an unusual choice — not 'news' or 'letters' but noticing the world on his behalf. It makes ordinary observation feel profound and generous.
  4. Why does the brother say 'they proved you were still there'?
    In isolation, the postcards were evidence of continuity — that Mia and her world persisted. They were proof of connection; in a remote place, that proof was essential and comforting.
  5. What theme does this passage explore?
    The importance of small, consistent acts of love and attention; how ordinary communication becomes extraordinary through accumulation; the loneliness of isolation and how it is quietly addressed.
Part 3 — Writing task
Write a short piece about a form of communication — a letter, postcard, phone call, or message — and what it meant beyond its literal content. Use: a specific real or imagined example, precise sensory detail, and a final line that reveals the deeper meaning.
My grandmother did not use the internet. Every fortnight, without fail, a letter arrived in her handwriting — blue ink on cream paper, three sides, ending with 'Your loving Nani.' She never said 'I love you' in those words. She said: 'The jasmine is out again this year' and 'Your uncle visited on Sunday and ate all the sweets.' But the letter itself was the I love you. The regularity of it, the three sides, the blue ink — these were its real content. When she died, there were forty-two of them in a box in my drawer. I have not opened the box since. I know what is in it.

Word of the Day

assiduous
Meaning: showing great care, attention, and dedication — working with constant, persistent effort.
Example: 'Mia's assiduous habit of sending a postcard each week was, in its own quiet way, a form of love.'
Challenge: Think of someone you know who is assiduous at something. Write one sentence describing them without using the word 'hard-working'.
