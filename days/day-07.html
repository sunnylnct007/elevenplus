Day 7

Probability, Fractions & Antarctica Comprehension

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 9 × 16?
    Answer: 144. Split: (9 × 10) + (9 × 6) = 90 + 54 = 144.
  2. Is 97 a prime number? How do you know?
    Answer: Yes. A prime has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Test all primes up to √97 ≈ 9.8: not divisible by 2, 3, 5, or 7. So 97 is prime.
  3. What is 2/3 + 3/4? Give your answer as a mixed number.
    Answer: 1 5/12. Common denominator is 12: 2/3 = 8/12, 3/4 = 9/12. Sum = 17/12 = 1 5/12.
  4. Convert 2.35 km into metres.
    Answer: 2,350 m. 1 km = 1,000 m, so 2.35 × 1,000 = 2,350 m.
  5. A bag contains 3 red, 5 blue, and 2 yellow counters. What is the probability of picking a blue counter at random?
    Answer: 1/2. Total counters = 3 + 5 + 2 = 10. Blue = 5. Probability = 5/10 = 1/2.
Probability strip — counters in the bag

5 Blue
2 Y

Blue takes up exactly half the strip → P(blue) = 5/10 = 1/2

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A train journey takes 3 hours 45 minutes. The train arrives at 17:20. What time did it depart?
    Answer: 13:35. Subtract 3 hours: 17:20 − 3 h = 14:20. Subtract 45 minutes: 14:20 − 45 min = 13:35.
  2. A farmer has 144 eggs. He packs them into boxes of 6. He sells each box for £1.80. How much does he earn in total?
    Answer: £43.20. Boxes = 144 ÷ 6 = 24 boxes. Earnings = 24 × £1.80 = £43.20.
  3. In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 2:3. There are 18 girls. How many boys are there?
    Answer: 12 boys. 3 parts = 18, so 1 part = 18 ÷ 3 = 6. Boys = 2 parts = 2 × 6 = 12. Check: 12:18 = 2:3 ✓
  4. A tank holds 60 litres when full. It is currently 3/5 full. How many more litres are needed to fill it?
    Answer: 24 litres. 3/5 of 60 = 36 litres already in the tank. Needed = 60 − 36 = 24 litres.
  5. Plot these coordinates and name the shape: A(1,1), B(5,1), C(5,4), D(1,4). What is its area?
    The shape is a rectangle. Width = 5 − 1 = 4 units. Height = 4 − 1 = 3 units. Area = 4 × 3 = 12 square units.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. Which sentence uses a semicolon correctly?
    A) She loved reading; especially fantasy novels.
    B) It was late; nevertheless, she kept working.
    C) The cat sat; on the warm windowsill.
    Answer: B. A semicolon joins two independent clauses (each could be a sentence). In A and C, the second part is not a full independent clause.
  2. Add the missing apostrophes:
    Its a shame that the childrens coats were left at the teachers house.
    Corrected: It’s a shame that the children’s coats were left at the teacher’s house. “It’s” = it is (contraction). “Children’s” = belonging to children (irregular plural). “Teacher’s” = belonging to one teacher.
  3. What is the difference between a noun phrase and a verb phrase? Give an example of each.
    A noun phrase is built around a noun: e.g. “the old, crumbling tower.” A verb phrase is built around a verb expressing action or state: e.g. “had been sleeping for hours.” Noun phrases can be expanded with adjectives, determiners, and prepositional phrases.
  4. What does the prefix mis- mean? Give two examples.
    “Mis-” means wrongly or badly. Examples: misunderstand (understand wrongly), misbehave (behave badly), mislead, misspell, misplace.
  5. What is onomatopoeia? Give two examples from nature or weather.
    Onomatopoeia is when a word sounds like the thing it describes. Nature/weather examples: “thunder rumbled”, “rain pattered”, “leaves rustled”, “waves crashed”, “wind howled”.

2. Short comprehension

The scientists had drilled for three weeks before they found it: a layer of ice that had not been touched by sunlight for two million years. Below it, according to their instruments, was liquid water. Not frozen. Liquid. Dr Yuen pressed her gloved hand against the drill casing and tried to imagine the ocean that might exist beneath her feet — dark, cold, and utterly silent. If there was water, there might be life. Simple life, perhaps: bacteria, microscopic creatures adapted to the crushing pressure and the permanent dark. “We need to go carefully,” she said to her team, her breath clouding in the frozen air. “Whatever is down there has been waiting two million years. Let’s not disturb it more than we have to.”
  1. How long had the scientists been drilling before they made their discovery?
    Answer: Three weeks.
  2. Why does the writer repeat “Liquid.” as a one-word sentence?
    The repetition and short sentence emphasise the shock and significance of the discovery. After “Not frozen.” another single word — “Liquid.” — lands with impact. The short sentences slow the reader down, as if Dr Yuen herself can barely believe what the instruments show.
  3. What might the discovery of liquid water suggest, according to Dr Yuen?
    It might suggest the presence of life — specifically simple life such as bacteria or microscopic creatures adapted to high pressure and darkness.
  4. What does the word adapted mean in this context?
    In this context, “adapted” means changed or evolved over time to suit particular conditions — here, the crushing pressure and permanent darkness. The creatures would be specially suited to survive in those extreme conditions.
  5. What does Dr Yuen’s final speech reveal about her character? Use evidence.
    She appears cautious, thoughtful, and respectful of the environment. She says “We need to go carefully” and “Let’s not disturb it more than we have to” — showing she is not just excited by discovery but concerned about human interference. This makes her seem like a responsible and ethical scientist.

3. Short writing task

Write a short paragraph or story opening (about 8–10 sentences):

“The team lowers a small camera into the water below the ice. Describe what the camera shows. What do they see? How do the scientists react?”

Try to use: present tense to create immediacy, precise scientific vocabulary alongside vivid description, and a dramatic final sentence.

Example answer:
The screen flickers and then resolves into a blue-black world of extraordinary stillness. Dr Yuen leans forward. The water is clear — far clearer than any ocean she has seen — and the camera’s light cuts through it in a perfect white cone. At first there is nothing: just water, and rock, and silence. Then she sees it. Something moves at the very edge of the light — a slow, pale pulse, rhythmic as breathing. “Is that…?” someone whispers behind her. Nobody finishes the sentence. The pulse moves again, unhurried and ancient, and the room holds its breath. Two million years of darkness, and now, for the first time, something is seen.

Check for: (1) present tense throughout: flickers, resolves, leans, moves (2) precise/scientific words: “resolves”, “extraordinary stillness”, “rhythmic” alongside vivid description (3) dramatic final sentence creating awe.

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking. Take your time.

  1. Mixed fraction problem: A jug holds 2 1/4 litres. A glass holds 3/8 of a litre. How many full glasses can be filled from the jug? How much water is left over?
    Convert 2 1/4 = 9/4 litres. Divide by 3/8: 9/4 ÷ 3/8 = 9/4 × 8/3 = 72/12 = 6 glasses exactly. No water left over.
  2. Probability & reasoning: A bag holds 4 red and n blue marbles. The probability of picking a red marble is 1/3. What is n?
    P(red) = 4/(4+n) = 1/3. Cross-multiply: 12 = 4+n, so n = 8. Check: 4/(4+8) = 4/12 = 1/3 ✓
  3. Time challenge: A film starts at 14:45 and lasts 2 hours 20 minutes. There is then a 15-minute interval before a second film of 1 hour 35 minutes. What time does the evening end?
    Film 1 ends: 14:45 + 2h 20min = 17:05. Interval ends: 17:05 + 15 min = 17:20. Film 2 ends: 17:20 + 1h 35min = 18:55.

Word of the Day

sentient
/ SEN-shent /  —  adjective
Able to feel, perceive, or experience things consciously; having awareness and sensation. A sentient being can sense its surroundings and respond to them with feeling.
“If bacteria had survived for two million years in the dark ocean, they could not be called sentient — yet they would be alive, and that alone was staggering.”
