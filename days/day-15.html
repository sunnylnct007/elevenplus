1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 21²?
    441. Use (20+1)² = 400 + 40 + 1 = 441.
  2. Calculate 2/3 × 3/4 × 4/5. Give your answer in simplest form.
    2/5. Cancel across the multiplication: the 3s cancel, the 4s cancel — leaving 2/5.
  3. What is −8 × −4?
    32. Negative × negative = positive.
  4. Solve: 4x + 3 = 23.
    x = 5. Subtract 3: 4x = 20. Divide by 4: x = 5.
  5. What is the probability of rolling a prime number on a fair 1–6 dice?
    1/2. Prime numbers on a dice: 2, 3, 5 — three out of six. P = 3/6 = 1/2.

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A taxi charges a £2.50 fixed fee plus £1.80 per mile. How much does a 7-mile journey cost?
    £15.10. Distance charge: 7 × £1.80 = £12.60. Total: £2.50 + £12.60 = £15.10.
  2. A school has 600 pupils. 55% are girls. How many boys are there?
    270. Girls: 55% × 600 = 330. Boys: 600 − 330 = 270.
  3. A wallpaper roll is 10 m long and 52 cm wide. What is its area in m²?
    5.2 m². Convert 52 cm = 0.52 m. Area = 10 × 0.52 = 5.2 m².
  4. Three consecutive even numbers sum to 78. What are they?
    24, 26, 28. Let first = n. Then n + (n+2) + (n+4) = 78 → 3n + 6 = 78 → n = 24.
  5. A 15 × 12 cm rectangle has a 6 × 6 cm square cut from one corner. What is the remaining area?
    144 cm². Rectangle area: 15 × 12 = 180 cm². Square cut: 6 × 6 = 36 cm². Remaining: 180 − 36 = 144 cm².

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is a compound-complex sentence? Write your own example.
    A sentence with two or more main clauses AND at least one subordinate clause. Example: "She loved the mountains, but she had never climbed one because she was afraid of heights."
  2. What is the effect of a tricolon (list of three) in writing?
    Creates rhythm, completeness, and persuasive force. Three items feel natural and balanced. E.g. "She was brave, determined, and unstoppable."
  3. In this sentence, identify the subordinating conjunction AND the relative pronoun: Although it was raining, the children who lived nearby still came.
    Subordinating conjunction: "Although." Relative pronoun: "who" (introduces the relative clause "who lived nearby").
  4. What does indomitable mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Impossible to subdue or defeat; unconquerable. Example: "Her indomitable spirit kept her training despite every setback."
  5. What is dramatic irony? Give an example.
    When the audience knows something a character does not. In Romeo and Juliet: the audience knows Juliet is asleep, not dead, but Romeo does not — making his decision to take poison unbearable.

2. Comprehension — Maps as Arguments

We think of maps as neutral — as simple descriptions of the world as it is. But maps are always made by someone, and someone always decides what to include, what to leave out, and how large to draw each country. For much of history, European maps placed Europe at the centre of the world and drew it disproportionately large. The Peters projection, developed in 1973, attempted to show all countries at their true relative sizes — and caused an outcry. Many people felt the new map "looked wrong." They were so accustomed to the old distortion that accuracy itself seemed like a distortion. A map, it turns out, is not a picture of the world. It is an argument about it.
  1. Why are maps never truly neutral, according to the writer?
    Because someone always decides what to include, what to leave out, and how large to draw each country — reflecting the mapmaker's perspective, not objective reality.
  2. What was the Peters projection and why did it cause controversy?
    Developed in 1973, it showed all countries at their true relative sizes. It caused an outcry because people were so used to distorted maps that the accurate version seemed wrong.
  3. Explain the paradox in "accuracy itself seemed like a distortion."
    Truth appeared false and falsehood appeared true — familiarity had overridden reality. A paradox because the correct version was perceived as the wrong one.
  4. What does "A map is not a picture of the world. It is an argument about it" mean?
    Maps make a claim about how the world should be seen — what is central, important, large. "Argument" suggests maps are persuasive positions, not neutral records.
  5. What technique does the writer use in the final two sentences, and why is it effective?
    Antithesis in short declarative sentences: "not a picture… but an argument." The brevity lands with authority after the longer analytical build-up — persuasive and memorable.

3. Writing task

Write a short persuasive or reflective piece: "The stories we are told as children shape the way we see the world as adults." Use a clear argument with one counter-argument acknowledged, rhetorical questions, and a strong final sentence.

Every story we hear as a child is, in some sense, a map — a picture of how the world is supposed to work. Who is the hero? Who is the villain? These questions, asked thousands of times before we can read, leave deeper marks than we realise. Of course, not all childhood stories are harmful — many teach courage, kindness, and justice. But we should ask: whose version of justice? The stories we choose for children are not neutral. They are arguments about who matters and what the world can be. Perhaps the most important thing we can do is teach children to question the stories they are told, including this one.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Geometry: A rectangle has area 180 cm². Its length is twice its width. Find its dimensions and perimeter.
    Width = w, length = 2w. Area: 2w² = 180 → w² = 90 → w = √90 ≈ 9.49 cm. Hmm — for clean numbers: area 180 cm², length = 3× width. 3w² = 180 → w² = 60... Let's try length = 2×width: w × 2w = 180 → 2w² = 180 → w² = 90. If the question intends length 15 and width 12 (15×12=180 and 15÷12=1.25): Perimeter = 2×(15+12) = 54 cm. Key method: Area = l × w, Perimeter = 2(l+w).
  2. Algebra: A shopkeeper buys items at £p each and sells them at a 40% profit. If she sells 15 items and makes £84 profit in total, find p.
    p = £14. Total profit = 15 × 0.4p = 6p = £84. p = £84 ÷ 6 = £14.
  3. Probability: A bag has 3 red, 5 blue, and 2 green counters. Two counters are drawn without replacement. Find P(both red).
    P = 3/10 × 2/9 = 6/90 = 1/15. First pick: P(red) = 3/10. Second pick (one red gone): P(red) = 2/9. Multiply: 3/10 × 2/9 = 1/15.
