📐 Maths (~20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. Solve: 5(2x − 3) = 25.
    x = 4. Expand: 10x − 15 = 25. Add 15: 10x = 40. Divide: x = 4.
  2. Express 0.125 as a fraction in simplest form.
    1/8. 0.125 = 125/1000 = 1/8. (Also: 0.125 = 1 ÷ 8.)
  3. A regular pentagon has perimeter 65 cm. What is the length of one side?
    13 cm. A pentagon has 5 equal sides. 65 ÷ 5 = 13 cm.
  4. What is the sum of all integers from 1 to 10?
    55. Use Gauss's method: pair 1+10=11, 2+9=11, 3+8=11, 4+7=11, 5+6=11. Five pairs of 11 = 55.
  5. A triangle has angles 47° and 68°. Find the third angle and classify the triangle.
    65°. Angles sum to 180°: 180 − 47 − 68 = 65°. All angles < 90° → acute triangle. All sides different → scalene.

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. In a pie chart, 45 out of 120 students chose science as their favourite subject. What angle represents this on the pie chart?
    135°. Fraction: 45/120 = 3/8. Angle: 3/8 × 360° = 135°.
  2. A cuboid has dimensions 1.5 m × 0.8 m × 1.2 m. Find its volume in litres (1 m³ = 1,000 litres).
    1,440 litres. Volume = 1.5 × 0.8 × 1.2 = 1.44 m³. In litres: 1.44 × 1000 = 1,440 L.
  3. Find the nth term of the sequence: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, …
    4n − 1. Common difference = 4 (so coefficient of n is 4). When n=1: 4(1)−1 = 3. ✓ When n=2: 4(2)−1 = 7. ✓
  4. A jacket is sold for £63 after a 30% discount. What was the original price?
    £90. £63 = 70% of original. Original = £63 ÷ 0.7 = £90.
  5. A running track has two straight sections of 100 m each and two semicircular ends with diameter 50 m. Find the total perimeter. (π = 3.14)
    357 m. Straight sections: 2 × 100 = 200 m. Two semicircles = one full circle: circumference = π × 50 = 3.14 × 50 = 157 m. Total: 200 + 157 = 357 m.

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. Rewrite this sentence in the passive voice: The council built the new library in 2019.
    "The new library was built by the council in 2019." In the passive, the object becomes the subject and the verb uses "to be" + past participle. The agent (the council) is introduced with "by" and can be omitted: "The new library was built in 2019."
  2. Convert this direct speech to reported speech: "We are leaving tomorrow," said Mia.
    Mia said that they were leaving the following day. Changes: "We" → "they"; "are leaving" → "were leaving" (backshift); "tomorrow" → "the following day."
  3. What does the prefix contra- mean? Give three words using it.
    Contra- means "against" or "opposite." Words: contradict (speak against), contrast (set against), contraband (against the ban), contraflow (opposite flow).
  4. What does ephemeral mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Lasting only a very short time; transitory. Example: "The beauty of cherry blossom is ephemeral — it fades within a week of opening."
  5. Name the four main types of imagery a writer can use and give one example of each.
    Visual: "The sky bled crimson." Auditory: "Leaves whispered in the wind." Tactile: "The cold stone bit into his palms." Olfactory: "The sharp scent of pine filled the air." Writers use multiple senses to make scenes vivid and immersive.

2. Comprehension — The Last Leaf

It had been clinging on for weeks — the last leaf on the oak at the bottom of the garden. While the others had let go in the first November gales, this one held on, dark and crumpled, its edges curled inward as if it were making a fist.

My grandmother watched it from the kitchen window every morning. She did not say much about it. But once, stirring her tea, she remarked: "It's being defiant." I didn't know, then, what she meant. A leaf cannot be defiant. And yet I kept looking at it too.

It fell in January, two days before she died. I have thought about that leaf, on and off, for the whole of my adult life. Not because I believe in signs. Because some things, small and unremarkable, teach you what you couldn't have learned from anything larger.

  1. How does the writer describe the last leaf, and what does this suggest?
    "Dark and crumpled, its edges curled inward as if it were making a fist." The simile of a fist gives the leaf a sense of will and resistance — it is fighting to hold on. This physical description prepares us for the grandmother's word "defiant."
  2. What does the grandmother mean when she says the leaf is "being defiant"?
    She is projecting human qualities onto the leaf — seeing in its stubborn survival a form of resistance against the season, against the inevitable. She may be thinking of herself as much as the leaf.
  3. What is the significance of the timing — the leaf fell two days before she died?
    It creates a symbolic connection between the leaf's final surrender and the grandmother's death. The writer does not claim it is a sign ("Not because I believe in signs"), but acknowledges the leaf as a powerful, lasting image nonetheless.
  4. What is the effect of the short final sentence: "Because some things, small and unremarkable, teach you what you couldn't have learned from anything larger."?
    It offers a quiet reflection — that small, ordinary things can carry profound meaning. The paradox (small teaches what large cannot) gives the sentence weight. It also justifies why the writer has told this story.
  5. How does the writer use the leaf as a symbol throughout the passage?
    The leaf begins as a physical object (described vividly), becomes a symbol of defiance through the grandmother's word, and ends as a symbol of human resilience and mortality. Its fall mirrors her death. The leaf carries meaning without the writer ever stating it directly — it is shown, not told.

3. Writing task

Choose a natural object (a stone, a wave, a bare tree, a cloud) and write a short piece in which it becomes a symbol for a human quality — without ever saying so directly.

Try to use: a specific sensory description, one person who notices the object, and a final line that widens the meaning quietly.

There is a stone on the windowsill that my father brought back from a beach in Wales thirty years ago. It is smooth and grey, palm-sized, unremarkable. He put it there the year he gave up drinking, and nobody has moved it since. When I visit, I notice it before I notice anything else. My father doesn't talk about that year. He just makes tea and asks about the children, and the stone sits in the window in the late afternoon light. It has been exactly the same, all this time. So has he.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Algebra: Solve: 3(x + 4) − 2(x − 1) = 20.
    x = 6. Expand: 3x + 12 − 2x + 2 = 20. Simplify: x + 14 = 20. x = 6.
  2. Percentage: A price rises 20% then falls 20%. What is the overall percentage change?
    −4% (a decrease). Start at £100. After +20%: £120. After −20%: £120 × 0.8 = £96. Net change: £96 − £100 = −£4, so 4% decrease. Successive percentage changes are NOT additive.
  3. nth term: The nth term of a sequence is n² + 3. List the first five terms and find which term equals 147.
    Terms: 4, 7, 12, 19, 28 (for n = 1,2,3,4,5). For 147: n² + 3 = 147 → n² = 144 → n = 12. The 12th term is 147.

💬 Word of the Day

resilient
adjective
Able to recover quickly from difficulties; capable of springing back to shape after being stretched or compressed. From Latin resilire — to spring back.
"She was resilient enough to return to training three weeks after the injury that had ended other careers."
