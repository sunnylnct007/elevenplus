2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A recipe needs 300 g flour for 12 biscuits. How much flour for 20 biscuits?
    500 g. 300 ÷ 12 = 25 g per biscuit. 25 × 20 = 500 g.
  2. A school trip costs £18.50 per pupil for 32 pupils. What is the total cost?
    £592. 32 × £18.50 = £592.
  3. A bag has 4 red, 6 blue, and 5 yellow marbles. What is P(red or yellow)?
    3/5. Favourable: 4 + 5 = 9. Total: 15. P = 9/15 = 3/5.
  4. Plot these coordinates and name the shape: (1,1), (4,1), (4,5), (1,5).
    Rectangle. Width = 3 units, height = 4 units. All four right angles; opposite sides equal. Since 3 ≠ 4, it is a rectangle not a square.
  5. A shop marks up goods by 35% on cost. Cost price is £60. What is the selling price?
    £81. 35% of £60 = £21. Selling price = £60 + £21 = £81.

📖 English (~20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is an oxymoron? Give two examples.
    An oxymoron places contradictory words together for effect. Examples: "bittersweet", "deafening silence", "living death". The contradiction creates a vivid, paradoxical meaning.
  2. Add a colon and a semi-colon correctly: She had one aim to win and she would stop at nothing to achieve it.
    She had one aim: to win; and she would stop at nothing to achieve it. Colon after "aim" introduces what the aim is. Semi-colon separates two closely related main clauses.
  3. What is the difference between first, second, and third person narration?
    First person: narrator is a character, uses "I/we" — intimate, subjective. Second person: addresses reader as "you" — unusual, immersive. Third person: narrator outside the story ("he/she/they") — can be omniscient (knows all) or limited (one character's view).
  4. What does labyrinthine mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Like a labyrinth; extremely complicated and full of twisting passages. Example: "The labyrinthine rules of the tax system confused even the most experienced accountants."
  5. What is an extended metaphor? How does it differ from a simple metaphor?
    A simple metaphor makes one comparison ("Life is a journey"). An extended metaphor develops that comparison across several sentences: "Life is a journey; some roads are smooth, others rutted with hardship; not all of us arrive where we intended." The same vehicle is revisited repeatedly.

2. Comprehension — The Chess Lesson

The summer I turned twelve, my grandmother taught me to play chess. She was not gentle about it. She did not let me win, not even once, and when I made a poor move she would tap the offending piece with one finger and say, simply, "Think." The board was old — the white squares had gone the colour of weak tea — and the pieces were mismatched, some belonging to a set long since lost. We played every evening after dinner, in the hour before the light faded. When I finally beat her, near the end of that summer, she studied the board for a long time after I said checkmate. Then she looked up and smiled and said: "Good. Now you can learn." I didn't understand that until much later.
  1. What did the narrator's grandmother teach that summer, and how did she teach it?
    Chess — not gently: refusing to let the narrator win, simply saying "Think" when a poor move was made.
  2. What details describe the chess set, and what do they suggest?
    White squares gone the colour of weak tea; mismatched pieces from a set long lost. These suggest the set is old and well-used — an object with history, implying the grandmother has been playing for a long time.
  3. Why is the final line — "I didn't understand that until much later" — effective?
    It creates retrospective irony: the narrator (now older) knows something their younger self did not. It suggests the lesson was about more than chess — mastery, humility, or life. The reader is invited to wonder what the real lesson was.
  4. What does the grandmother mean by "Good. Now you can learn."?
    Winning was not the goal — it was only the beginning. Real learning starts after you can beat a teacher, when you must push beyond what they gave you. The remark reframes victory as merely a threshold, not an achievement.
  5. How does the writer use time in this passage?
    The whole summer is compressed into a few key moments. The final sentence leaps forward to adulthood. This compression and leap gives the passage the quality of memory: vivid in some moments, hazy in the connections.

3. Writing task

Write a short piece about something a person taught you — not a school lesson but something you understood only later. Use: a specific person doing a specific thing, a moment of apparent failure or confusion, and a final line from the perspective of your older self.

My father never explained anything twice. He showed you once — how to change a tyre, how to read a map — and then he waited. When I did it wrong, he watched. I found this infuriating for most of my childhood. Now I understand: he was teaching me to be patient with myself, to try again without expecting rescue, to solve things without asking someone else first. He never said any of this. He just watched, and waited. I am still learning to do the same.

⭐ Bonus Maths Challenge

  1. Algebra: The sum of three consecutive multiples of 5 is 150. What are they?
    45, 50, 55. Let them be 5n, 5n+5, 5n+10. Sum = 15n+15 = 150 → 15n = 135 → n = 9. So: 45, 50, 55.
  2. Percentages: A coat costs £85 after a 15% discount. What was the original price?
    £100. £85 = 85% of original. Original = £85 ÷ 0.85 = £100.
  3. Coordinates: A rectangle has vertices at (2, 1), (8, 1), and (8, 5). What are the coordinates of the fourth vertex? Find the perimeter and area.
    Fourth vertex: (2, 5). Width = 8−2 = 6 units, height = 5−1 = 4 units. Perimeter = 2×(6+4) = 20 units. Area = 6×4 = 24 square units.

💬 Word of the Day

sagacious
adjective
Having or showing keen mental discernment and good judgement; wise. From Latin sagax — of quick perception.
"The grandmother's sagacious teaching style — never letting her grandchild win — proved, in the end, the greater kindness."
