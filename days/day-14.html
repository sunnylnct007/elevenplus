Day 14

Algebra, Average Speed & the Arctic Tern

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 36 × 15?
    Answer: 540. 36 × 10 = 360, 36 × 5 = 180. Sum = 540.
  2. Simplify: 3x + 5x − 2x
    Answer: 6x. Collect like terms: 3 + 5 − 2 = 6.
  3. What is 1/3 of 4/5?
    Answer: 4/15. “Of” means multiply: 1/3 × 4/5 = 4/15.
  4. A number is tripled then 8 is subtracted; the result is 28. What is the number?
    Answer: 12. Work backwards: 28 + 8 = 36. 36 ÷ 3 = 12. Check: 12 × 3 = 36, 36 − 8 = 28 ✓
  5. What is the area of a triangle with base 14 cm and height 9 cm?
    Answer: 63 cm². A = ½ × 14 × 9 = 63 cm².
Speed, Distance, Time triangle

Cover what you want: D = S × T  |  S = D ÷ T  |  T = D ÷ S

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. Pens cost 45p and rulers cost 80p. Jess spends exactly £5. Find one possible combination.
    4 rulers + 4 pens = (4 × 80p) + (4 × 45p) = 320p + 180p = 500p = £5. ✓ (Other combinations may also work.)
  2. A train travels 120 km at 80 km/h, then returns at 60 km/h. What is the total journey time?
    Outward: 120 ÷ 80 = 1.5 h. Return: 120 ÷ 60 = 2 h. Total = 3.5 hours.
  3. 30 pupils have a mean score of 64. Two pupils scored 100. What is the mean of the remaining 28?
    Total score = 64 × 30 = 1,920. Remove two 100s: 1,920 − 200 = 1,720. Mean of 28 = 1,720 ÷ 28 ≈ 61.4.
  4. An isosceles triangle has two equal angles of 68°. What is the third angle?
    Answer: 44°. 180 − 68 − 68 = 44°.
  5. A price increases by 15% from £260. What is the new amount?
    Answer: £299. 10% of £260 = £26. 5% = £13. 15% = £39. £260 + £39 = £299.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is the pluperfect (past perfect) tense? Give an example.
    The pluperfect uses had + past participle for an action completed before another past event. Example: “She realised she had left her keys at home.” (“had left” happened before “realised”.)
  2. Identify and correct the dangling modifier:
    Running down the street, the bus disappeared from sight.
    The modifier “running” should describe a person, not the bus. Corrected: “Running down the street, he watched the bus disappear from sight.” The modifier must be next to the noun it describes.
  3. What is the difference between formal and informal register? Give an example of each.
    Formal: complete sentences, sophisticated vocabulary, no contractions. Informal: conversational, contractions, colloquial. Same idea — Formal: “I would be grateful if you could attend.” Informal: “Can you come?”
  4. What does poignant mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Poignant means evoking keen sadness, especially for something beautiful but fading. Example: “The final photograph was particularly poignant, taken just days before they parted.”
  5. What are the three degrees of comparison? Use the word cold.
    Positive: cold. Comparative: colder. Superlative: coldest. For longer adjectives: beautiful → more beautiful → most beautiful.

2. Short comprehension

The Arctic tern holds a record that no other creature on Earth can match. Each year, it migrates from its breeding grounds near the North Pole to the Antarctic and back again — a round trip of up to 90,000 kilometres. To put this in perspective: over the course of its lifetime, an Arctic tern may travel the equivalent of three trips to the Moon and back. It weighs roughly 100 grams — less than a bar of chocolate. It navigates using the sun, the stars, and the Earth’s magnetic field. It sleeps in flight. And yet, despite this extraordinary endurance, the Arctic tern is rarely celebrated. It is not majestic, like the eagle. It is not beloved, like the robin. It is small, loud, and surprisingly aggressive — but it crosses the world twice a year as a matter of routine, and has done so for millions of years.
  1. What record does the Arctic tern hold?
    It migrates further than any other creature — up to 90,000 km per year, from Arctic to Antarctic and back.
  2. What is the purpose of the “bar of chocolate” and “three trips to the Moon” comparisons?
    Both make abstract facts concrete and surprising. “Less than a bar of chocolate” highlights the bird’s tiny weight; “three trips to the Moon” gives lifetime distance a relatable scale.
  3. How does the writer use contrast throughout?
    Tiny weight vs enormous distance; unrecognised vs extraordinary; “not majestic… not beloved” vs record-breaking migration. Appearance vs hidden achievement is the writer’s key argument.
  4. What is the writer’s tone and attitude? How can you tell?
    Admiring and gently critical. “Extraordinary endurance” shows admiration; “rarely celebrated” suggests absurdity. “Has done so for millions of years” carries quiet awe.
  5. What wider point might the writer be making?
    We celebrate surface qualities (beauty, majesty) over substance (endurance, quiet achievement). The tern symbolises unrecognised excellence.

3. Short writing task

Write a short piece (about 8–10 sentences):

“Choose an animal, person, or thing that is underestimated or overlooked. Write a short piece revealing why it deserves more recognition.”

Try to use: surprising comparisons, contrast between appearance and achievement, and a strong final sentence.

Example answer (the earthworm):
The earthworm has no eyes, no ears, and no skeleton. It is, by almost any measure, unremarkable — and yet without it, most of the food on your plate would not exist. A single acre of healthy farmland may contain over a million earthworms. Charles Darwin spent 44 years studying them and concluded they had shaped more of Earth’s surface than any other animal. They do not appear on nature documentaries. They are simply there, beneath your feet, doing the quiet, essential work of keeping the world alive. If that is not extraordinary, it is hard to say what is.

Check for: (1) surprising comparisons: Darwin’s 44 years, 1 million per acre (2) contrast: unremarkable appearance vs essential function (3) strong final sentence.

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking.

  1. Average speed trap: A car travels 60 km at 40 km/h, then 60 km at 60 km/h. What is the average speed for the whole journey? (Beware: it is NOT 50 km/h.)
    Time for first half: 60÷40 = 1.5 h. Time for second half: 60÷60 = 1 h. Total time = 2.5 h. Total distance = 120 km. Average speed = 120÷2.5 = 48 km/h. Not 50 — the slower speed takes more time, pulling the average down.
  2. Algebra investigation: Solve: 5(2x − 3) = 35. Then verify your answer by substituting back.
    Expand: 10x − 15 = 35. 10x = 50. x = 5. Check: 5(2×5 − 3) = 5(10−3) = 5×7 = 35 ✓
  3. Mean challenge: A class of 20 has a mean score of 72. A class of 30 has a mean score of 66. What is the combined mean?
    Total for class A: 20 × 72 = 1,440. Total for class B: 30 × 66 = 1,980. Combined total = 3,420. Combined pupils = 50. Mean = 3,420 ÷ 50 = 68.4. (Not 69 — the larger class pulls the average down.)

Word of the Day

indefatigable
/ in-deh-FAT-ih-guh-bul /  —  adjective
Persisting tirelessly, never showing signs of becoming exhausted or giving up. An indefatigable person or creature keeps going long after others would have stopped.
“The Arctic tern is perhaps the most indefatigable traveller on Earth — crossing 90,000 kilometres every year, not as an achievement, but as a matter of routine.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using indefatigable to describe a person, animal, or historical figure. Can you think of a real person in history who could be described as indefatigable?
