Day 13

Trapeziums, Speed & The Music Room

Maths · English · Bonus Challenge · Word of the Day

🕑 Maths: 20 min 🕑 English: 20 min ★ Bonus: 10 min
✎

Maths (about 20 minutes)

1. Core skills warm-up

  1. What is 48 × 25?
    Answer: 1,200. Trick: 48 × 25 = 48 × 100 ÷ 4 = 4,800 ÷ 4 = 1,200.
  2. What is 4/5 ÷ 2/3?
    Answer: 6/5 = 1 1/5. Dividing by a fraction = multiply by its reciprocal: 4/5 × 3/2 = 12/10 = 6/5.
  3. What is 7.5% of 240?
    Answer: 18. 5% of 240 = 12. 2.5% of 240 = 6. Total = 18.
  4. A regular polygon has an exterior angle of 45°. How many sides does it have?
    Answer: 8 sides. Exterior angles sum to 360°. 360 ÷ 45 = 8 sides. (This is an octagon.)
  5. What is the next term in the sequence: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, ___?
    Answer: 49. These are square numbers (1², 2², 3², …). The next is 7² = 49.
Trapezium — area formula

Formula: A = ½ × (a + b) × h   where a and b are the parallel sides

2. Word problems & exam-style practice

  1. A price increased by 20% to £84. What was the original price?
    Answer: £70. £84 = 120% of original. Original = £84 ÷ 1.2 = £70.
  2. A trapezium has parallel sides 12 m and 8 m, and height 5 m. What is its area?
    Answer: 50 m². A = ½ × (12 + 8) × 5 = ½ × 20 × 5 = 50 m².
  3. Share £360 in the ratio 3:4:5. How much does each person receive?
    Total parts = 3 + 4 + 5 = 12. One part = £360 ÷ 12 = £30. Shares: £90, £120, £150.
  4. What is the angle between the hands of a clock at 3:15?
    Answer: 7.5°. Minute hand at 90° (3 o’clock position). Hour hand moves 0.5° per minute past 90°: 0.5 × 15 = 7.5°. So hour hand is at 97.5°. Difference = 7.5°.
  5. A cyclist covers 45 km in 1 hour 30 minutes. What is the average speed in km/h?
    Answer: 30 km/h. 1 h 30 min = 1.5 h. Speed = 45 ÷ 1.5 = 30 km/h.

English (about 20 minutes)

1. SPaG & vocabulary

  1. What is a modal verb? List four examples and explain what each expresses.
    Modal verbs express possibility, ability, obligation, or permission. Examples: can (ability), might (weak possibility), must (strong obligation), should (advice), would (conditional), could (past ability or polite request).
  2. Correct all errors:
    “Your going to love there new house,” she said, “its even bigger then the last one.”
    Corrected: “You’re going to love their new house,” she said, “it’s even bigger than the last one.” Errors: your→you’re; there→their; its→it’s; then→than.
  3. What is a complex sentence? Write one.
    A complex sentence has a main clause + at least one subordinate clause joined by a subordinating conjunction. Example: “Although she had studied for weeks, she still felt nervous as she walked in.”
  4. What does trepidation mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Trepidation means fear or anxiety about what might happen. Example: “With trepidation, she pushed open the door to her new school.”
  5. What is the purpose of a rhetorical question in persuasive writing?
    Asked for effect, not an answer — draws the reader in and implies the answer is obvious. Example: “Do we really want our children to grow up in a polluted world?”

2. Short comprehension

The first thing Anya noticed when she entered the old music room was that it smelled of time. Not dust exactly — something more specific: rosin, and old wood, and the particular metallic tang of piano strings that had been silent for too long. A cello stood in the corner, alone on its stand, as if it had simply been waiting for someone to return. She had been told that the room had been locked for twelve years, ever since the school’s music teacher — a formidable woman named Miss Hargreaves — had retired without warning and never come back. Nobody had thought to open it since. Anya crossed to the cello and placed her hand flat against its shoulder. Beneath her palm, the wood was warm, as though it still held the memory of music.
  1. What details suggest the room has been unused?
    Locked for twelve years; piano strings silent for too long; nobody had reopened it since Miss Hargreaves left.
  2. How does smell create atmosphere in this passage?
    Specific layered smells (rosin, old wood, metallic strings) make the room vivid and real. “Smelled of time” is a metaphor signalling age and stillness — smell is the most evocative sense for memory.
  3. What is the effect of “still held the memory of music”?
    It personifies the cello — gives it a kind of inner life, as if music has left a trace. The warmth becomes mysterious and emotional rather than merely physical.
  4. What do we learn about Miss Hargreaves? What is left unanswered?
    She was “formidable”, left without warning, and never returned. Unanswered: why she left, where she went, and why no one reopened the room for twelve years.
  5. How does the writer make the cello feel like more than an instrument? Give two techniques.
    (1) Personification: “waiting for someone to return” — as if it had expectations. (2) Metaphor: “held the memory of music” — suggests it retains past experience. (3) Body language: Anya places her hand on its “shoulder” — treating it like a person.

3. Short writing task

Write a short piece (about 8–10 sentences):

“Anya picks up the cello and begins to play. Describe the first few notes and what happens in the room as the music starts.”

Try to use: personification of the cello, pathetic fallacy, and a sense of awakening.

Example answer:
The first note was reluctant — thin and uncertain, as if the cello itself was remembering how to speak. Then the bow found its grip, and the sound opened like a door long shut. Outside, the rain that had been falling all morning seemed to pause. She played on — not a piece she knew, but something that seemed to come from the instrument itself. In the corridor, a teacher stopped walking. Music had been waiting all along, held in the walls like a held breath, waiting only for someone to release it.

Check for: (1) personification: cello “remembering how to speak” (2) pathetic fallacy: rain pausing as music begins (3) awakening: the corridor, the teacher stopping, the walls releasing.

Bonus Maths Challenge

Three harder problems to stretch your thinking.

  1. Reverse percentage: After a 15% increase, a price is £92. What was the original price? After a further 10% decrease, what is the final price?
    Original: £92 ÷ 1.15 = £80. Final after 10% decrease: £92 × 0.9 = £82.80.
  2. Compound area: A shape is made of a rectangle (10 m × 4 m) with a trapezium on top (parallel sides 10 m and 6 m, height 3 m). What is the total area?
    Rectangle: 10 × 4 = 40 m². Trapezium: ½ × (10 + 6) × 3 = ½ × 16 × 3 = 24 m². Total = 64 m².
  3. Clock problem: At what time between 4 and 5 o’clock are the hands of the clock exactly together?
    At 4:00 the hour hand is at 120° and minute hand at 0°. The minute hand gains 5.5° per minute on the hour hand. For the minute hand to catch up 120°: 120 ÷ 5.5 = 21.8 minutes ≈ 4:21 and 49 seconds.

Word of the Day

resonant
/ REZ-uh-nunt /  —  adjective
Producing a deep, rich sound that continues to vibrate. Also used figuratively to mean something that evokes a strong emotional response or has lasting significance — an idea or image can be resonant if it stays in the mind long after.
“The first note Anya drew from the cello was unexpectedly resonant — it filled the room and seemed to vibrate in the walls as if the space itself had been waiting to speak.”
Your turn: Write a sentence using resonant in a figurative sense (not about sound, but about an idea or memory). What is something that has stayed resonant with you long after you experienced it?
