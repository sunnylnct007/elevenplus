Maths

Part 1 — Core skills warm-up
  1. Solve: x² − x − 12 = 0.
    x = 4 or x = −3. Factorise: (x − 4)(x + 3) = 0 → x = 4 or x = −3.
  2. What is 2⁰ + 2¹ + 2² + 2³ + 2⁴?
    31. 1 + 2 + 4 + 8 + 16 = 31.
  3. A line is perpendicular to y = 3x + 1. What is its gradient?
    −1/3. Perpendicular gradients multiply to −1. m × 3 = −1 → m = −1/3.
  4. Expand: (2x + 3)².
    4x² + 12x + 9. (2x + 3)(2x + 3) = 4x² + 6x + 6x + 9 = 4x² + 12x + 9.
  5. Express 3/7 as a decimal to 3 significant figures.
    0.429. 3 ÷ 7 = 0.42857… ≈ 0.429 (3 s.f.).
Part 2 — Word problems & exam-style practice
  1. A square-based pyramid has a 10 × 10 cm base and a height of 12 cm. Find its volume.
    400 cm³. V = ⅓ × base area × height = ⅓ × 100 × 12 = 400 cm³.
  2. A train travels 196 km in 2 hours 20 minutes. What is its average speed in km/h?
    84 km/h. 2h 20min = 7/3 hours. Speed = 196 ÷ (7/3) = 196 × 3/7 = 84 km/h.
  3. In a class of 120 pupils: 45 like art, 60 like sport, 20 like both. How many like neither?
    35. Union = 45 + 60 − 20 = 85. Neither = 120 − 85 = 35.
Union = 45 + 60 − 20 = 85 → Neither = 120 − 85 = 35
  1. A price increases by 10%, then by a further 10%. What is the total percentage increase?
    21%. 1.1 × 1.1 = 1.21 → a 21% increase. NOT 20% — compound percentage growth means each rise acts on the new total.
  2. Prove that (n + 1)² − n² = 2n + 1 for all integers n.
    (n+1)² − n²
    = n² + 2n + 1 − n²
    = 2n + 1 ✓ QED
    This proves consecutive square numbers always differ by successive odd numbers (1, 3, 5, 7…).

English

Part 1 — SPaG & vocabulary
  1. What is intertextuality? Give an example.
    Intertextuality is when a text deliberately references or echoes another text. E.g. Shakespeare references Greek mythology; modern novels echo Dickens. It creates layers of meaning and rewards readers with wider knowledge.
  2. What is the difference between theme and subject in a text?
    Subject = what the text is literally about (a war, a family). Theme = the abstract idea explored through the subject (cost of loyalty, loss of innocence). E.g. Subject: a boy at boarding school. Theme: the loss of childhood freedom.
  3. What is stream of consciousness as a narrative technique?
    It captures the continuous, unfiltered flow of a character's thoughts — fragmented and associative, without formal punctuation. Associated with Virginia Woolf and James Joyce. E.g. 'She walked to the window — the curtains were blue — James used to hate blue — where was he now — the tea had gone cold.'
  4. What does ineffable mean? Use it in a sentence.
    Ineffable means too great or extreme to be expressed in words; beyond description. E.g. 'There was something ineffable about standing at the summit — a feeling beyond language, beyond the word magnificent.'
  5. Describe five key features of excellent 11+ writing.
    1Varied sentence structure: short for impact, complex for depth — contrast is powerful.
    2Precise vocabulary: choose the exact word, not a vague one. 'Crept' beats 'walked quietly'.
    3Show, don't tell: concrete details imply emotions rather than stating them.
    4Original imagery: fresh metaphors, not clichés. Avoid 'as quiet as a mouse'.
    5Strong opening and closing: first and last sentences carry disproportionate weight — make them count.
Part 2 — Comprehension: Just There. Just Certain.
On the last day of term, the school gave us each a sheet of paper and asked us to write our name on it. That was all. Just our name. We were told to fold it and put it in our pocket. At the time I thought it was a strange and pointless exercise, the sort of thing teachers invent when they have run out of ideas. I have thought about it many times since. The act of writing your own name — full name, carefully — is an odd one. You are asserting something. You exist, you have a name, and today, for no particular reason, you have been asked to confirm it. I still have the paper, somewhere in a box, aged now, the ink faded. My name, fourteen years old, not sure yet what it would become. Just there. Just certain.
  1. What were the pupils asked to do?
    Write their own name on a sheet of paper, fold it, and put it in their pocket.
  2. How did the narrator feel about the exercise at the time?
    Strange and pointless — 'the sort of thing teachers invent when they have run out of ideas.'
  3. What does the narrator say writing your name involves?
    An assertion: 'You exist, you have a name... you have been asked to confirm it.' A quiet, deliberate act of self-declaration.
  4. What is the effect of 'aged now, the ink faded'?
    Time has passed; the paper has changed just as the narrator has. The fading ink makes memory feel fragile and physical — contrasting with the certainty of the final two sentences.
  5. Analyse the final two sentences: 'Just there. Just certain.'
    Short declarative fragments echo the simplicity of the original act. The parallelism ('Just… Just') creates quiet, rhythmic balance. 'Certain' is the key word: the fourteen-year-old did not know what they would become — but the fact of existing was certain. Clean, complete, and resonant.
Part 3 — Final writing challenge
Choose ONE:

Option A (Narrative): Write the opening of a story where a small, ordinary object turns out to be unexpectedly significant.

Option B (Reflective): Write a short reflective piece about a small moment or gesture that has stayed with you. Show why it mattered through specific detail — do not state it directly.
Option B example: When I was nine, a stranger on a bus gave me her window seat without being asked. I had been standing for twenty minutes and she must have noticed. She just stood up and moved to an aisle seat, without looking at me, without expecting thanks. I did not say thank you. I was nine and did not yet know how. I have thought about it ever since — not because it was a large kindness, but because it was completely unprompted. She gained nothing. She did not need me to notice. It was, I think, the first time I understood that goodness could be this quiet: private, unannounced, requiring nothing in return.

Word of the Day

indelible
Meaning: making marks that cannot be removed or forgotten; leaving a permanent impression.
Example: 'The act of writing her name had left an indelible mark — not on the paper, which had faded, but on the person she had become.'
Challenge: Describe an indelible memory of your own in one sentence, without using the words 'remember', 'forget', or 'memory'.
