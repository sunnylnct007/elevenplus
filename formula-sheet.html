Maths — Number
Fractions
a/b + c/d = (ad+bc)/bd
Find a common denominator first. Simplify by dividing numerator and denominator by their HCF.
a/b × c/d = ac/bd
a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c
To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal.
Percentages
x% of n = n × x/100
% increase = (change ÷ original) × 100
Compound: A = P × (1 + r/100)ⁿ
P = principal, r = rate %, n = years
Standard Form
a × 10ⁿ (where 1 ≤ a < 10)
E.g. 36,000 = 3.6 × 10⁴  ·  0.0042 = 4.2 × 10⁻³
Ratio
Parts: total ÷ (a+b) × each part
E.g. ratio 3:5, total 40 → 1 part = 5 → shares = 15 and 25.
LCM & HCF
LCM = smallest common multiple
HCF = largest common factor
Use prime factor trees to find both efficiently. LCM × HCF = product of the two numbers.
Probability
P(event) = favourable ÷ total
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) [if mutually exclusive]
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) [if independent]
P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
Statistics
Mean = sum ÷ count
Median = middle value (ordered)
Mode = most frequent value
Range = max − min
Maths — Algebra
Linear Equations
y = mx + c
m = gradient, c = y-intercept. Gradient = rise ÷ run = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁).
y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
Equation of a line through (x₁, y₁) with gradient m.
Perpendicular gradient = −1/m
Quadratics
ax² + bx + c = 0
Factorise: find two numbers that multiply to c and add to b.
x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a
Quadratic formula — use when factorising doesn't work.
Sequences
nth term = dn + (a − d)
d = common difference, a = first term. E.g. 3,7,11… → d=4, nth term = 4n−1.
Simultaneous Equations
Method 1 (Substitution): rearrange one equation, substitute into the other.
Method 2 (Elimination): make coefficients equal, then add/subtract to cancel one variable.
Expanding & Factorising
a(b+c) = ab + ac
(a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
(a+b)(a−b) = a² − b²
Maths — Geometry
Areas
Rectangle = l × w
Triangle = ½ × b × h
Parallelogram = b × h
Trapezium = ½(a+b) × h
Circle = π r²
Sector = (θ/360) × π r²
Circumference & Perimeter
Circumference = 2πr = πd
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Semicircle perimeter = πr + 2r
Volumes
Cuboid = l × w × h
Cylinder = π r² h
Cone = ⅓ π r² h
Sphere = 4/3 π r³
Pyramid = ⅓ × base area × h
Pythagoras & Trigonometry
a² + b² = c²
c = hypotenuse (longest side, opposite right angle).
sin θ = opp/hyp
cos θ = adj/hyp
tan θ = opp/adj
Remember: SOH CAH TOA
Maths — Other Key Facts
Speed, Distance, Time
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
Distance = Speed × Time
Time = Distance ÷ Speed
Cover the variable you want with your thumb — the remaining two show the operation.
Bearings
Always measured clockwise from North
Back bearing = bearing + 180°
(If result > 360°, subtract 360°.)
Angle Facts
Angles in triangle = 180°
Angles on a straight line = 180°
Angles around a point = 360°
Interior angle of regular n-gon = (n−2)×180/n

Literary Techniques
Figurative Language
Simile Comparison using 'like' or 'as'. 'Her laugh was like a bell.'
Metaphor Direct comparison; states one thing IS another. 'He was a lion in battle.'
Personification Giving human qualities to non-human things. 'The wind whispered.'
Hyperbole Deliberate, dramatic exaggeration. 'I've told you a million times.'
Zoomorphism Giving animal qualities to humans/objects.
Oxymoron Contradictory words together. 'Deafening silence.'
Sound Devices
Alliteration Repetition of initial consonant sounds. 'Silent silver streams.'
Sibilance Repetition of 's'/'sh' sounds — can suggest stealth, calm, or menace.
Onomatopoeia Words that sound like their meaning. Crash, buzz, hiss.
Assonance Repetition of vowel sounds. 'Hear the mellow wedding bells.'
Consonance Repetition of consonant sounds within/end of words.
Structural & Rhetorical Devices
Anaphora Repetition of a word/phrase at the start of successive clauses. 'We shall fight… we shall never surrender.'
Tricolon Three parallel phrases in a row for rhythm and emphasis.
Chiasmus Reversed grammatical structures. 'Ask not what your country can do for you…'
Zeugma One verb governing two very different objects. 'He lost his keys and his temper.'
Polysyndeton Extra conjunctions for accumulating effect. 'She ran and jumped and laughed.'
Asyndeton Omitting conjunctions for speed. 'She ran, jumped, laughed.'
Bathos Abrupt drop from serious to trivial, for comic/ironic effect.
Litotes Understatement via double negative. 'Not bad at all.'
Narrative Techniques
1st person 'I' — intimate; we see through one character's eyes.
3rd person limited 'He/she' — one character's perspective, from outside.
3rd person omniscient All-knowing narrator; can enter any character's mind.
Unreliable narrator A narrator whose account we cannot fully trust.
Stream of consciousness Unfiltered, fragmented flow of a character's thoughts.
Dramatic irony When the reader knows something a character doesn't.
Prolepsis Hinting at future events; foreshadowing.
Cliffhanger Ending a section at an unresolved moment of tension.
Poetry Terms
Iambic pentameter 10 syllables per line: da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM. Used by Shakespeare.
Enjambment A sentence running over a line break without a pause.
Caesura A strong pause in the middle of a line.
Rhyme scheme Pattern of end-rhymes. ABAB, AABB, etc.
Grammar Techniques
Subordinate clause A clause that depends on a main clause to make sense. Introduced by: because, although, when, if, since…
Parenthetical clause Extra information inserted into a sentence, marked by brackets, dashes, or commas.
Passive voice The subject receives the action. 'The book was read by Maya' (vs. active: 'Maya read the book').
Subjunctive mood Expresses wishes, hypotheticals, demands. 'If I were you…' 'I suggest that he be told.'
Analytical Writing (PEA)
Point State your argument clearly in one sentence.
Evidence Quote from the text. Use inverted commas.
Analysis Explain the technique used and its effect on the reader. Use: 'This suggests…' / 'The writer uses X to convey…' / 'The effect of this is…'
Useful analysis phrases:
'The word "…" suggests…'
'The use of [technique] creates a sense of…'
'This implies that…'
'The reader is made to feel…'
'The contrast between… and… highlights…'
Key Vocabulary Distinctions
Easily Confused Terms
Explicit Stated directly in the text (you can quote it). Implicit Implied — you must infer it.
Theme The abstract idea (loyalty, loss). Subject What the text is literally about (a war, a family).
Sympathy Feeling sorry for a character from outside. Empathy Feeling what the character feels from inside.
Tone The writer's attitude (ironic, solemn). Mood The atmosphere created for the reader (tense, melancholy).
Denotation The literal meaning of a word. Connotation The associations and feelings it carries.
5 Features of Excellent 11+ Writing
1. Varied sentences Short for impact. Complex for depth. Contrast is powerful.
2. Precise vocabulary 'Crept' beats 'walked quietly'. Choose the exact word.
3. Show, don't tell Concrete details imply emotions rather than stating them.
4. Original imagery Fresh metaphors. Avoid: 'as quiet as a mouse', 'heart of gold'.
5. Strong opening & closing First and last sentences carry disproportionate weight — make them count.
