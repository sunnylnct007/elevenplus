Section 1 — Poetry Comprehension  [20 marks]
The poem below is based on a true story about Alexander Selkirk, who was marooned on a deserted island for several years. Read it carefully, then answer Questions 1–10. Each question is worth 2 marks — 1 for a correct point, 1 for expressing it in a full sentence.

The Solitude of Alexander Selkirk

by William Cowper
Stanza 1 I am monarch of all I survey; My right there is none to dispute; From the centre all round to the sea I am lord of the fowl and the brute. O Solitude! where are the charms That sages have seen in thy face? Better dwell in the midst of alarms, Than reign in this horrible place.
Stanza 2 I am out of humanity's reach; I must finish my journey alone; Never hear the sweet music of speech — I start at the sound of my own; The beasts that roam over the plain My form with indifference see — They are so unacquainted with man, Their tameness is shocking to me.
Stanza 3 Society, Friendship, and Love Divinely bestow'd upon man, Oh had I the wings of a dove How soon would I taste you again! My sorrows I then might assuage In the ways of religion and truth, Might learn from the wisdom of age, And be cheer'd by the sallies of youth.
Stanza 4 Ye winds that have made me your sport, Convey to this desolate shore Some cordial endearing report Of a land I shall visit no more. My friends, do they now and then send A wish or a thought after me? O tell me I yet have a friend, Though a friend I am never to see.
Stanza 5 How fleet is a glance of the mind! Compared with the speed of its flight, The tempest itself lags behind, And the swift-wingèd arrows of light. When I think of my own native land, In a moment I seem to be there; But, alas! recollection at hand Soon hurries me back to despair.
Stanza 6 But the sea-fowl is gone to her nest, The beast is laid down in his lair; Even here is a season of rest, And I to my cabin repair. There's mercy in every place; And mercy — encouraging thought! — Gives even affliction a grace, And reconciles man to his lot.
1.From the first stanza, how do we know that Selkirk is on an island?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: We know Selkirk is on an island because the poet writes “from the centre all round to the sea”, suggesting that wherever you stand on the land, the sea surrounds you completely.
2.Give one example from the poem that shows Selkirk is the only person on the island.[2]

ⓘ Model answer: In stanza 2 the poet writes “I am out of humanity's reach”, which tells us that Selkirk is completely cut off from all other human beings and is entirely alone on the island.
3.What is meant by “I must finish my journey alone”?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: This phrase means that Selkirk believes he will spend the rest of his life on the island without any human company, and that he must face his fate — and even death — without anyone beside him.
4.What effect does Selkirk's own voice have on him?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: Selkirk's own voice startles him when he hears it, as stated in “I start at the sound of my own”. This shows how eerie and unnerving the silence on the island is — he has become so unused to human speech that even his own voice surprises him.
5.If Selkirk had “the wings of a dove”, what would he do?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: If he had wings, Selkirk would fly away to rejoin society, friendship and love as quickly as possible. He longs to experience human companionship again and to find comfort through religion and the company of both old and young people.
6.In stanza 4, which word means “bleak” or “barren”?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The word is desolate (“Convey to this desolate shore”). It means that the shore is empty, bare, and utterly lifeless — emphasising how isolated and dreary Selkirk's surroundings are.
7.What does Selkirk wonder about his friends?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: Selkirk wonders whether his friends back home ever spare a thought for him, asking “My friends, do they now and then send / a wish or a thought after me?” He longs to know whether he is still remembered by those he loves.
8.Why do you think the poet repeats the word “friend/s” in stanza 4?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The poet repeats “friend/s” to emphasise how desperately Selkirk longs for human companionship. The repetition highlights the cruel irony of his situation: he is crying out for a friend, yet he knows he will never see one. It gives the stanza a sense of sadness and yearning.
9.What causes Selkirk to “despair” at the end of stanza 5?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: When Selkirk thinks of his native land, his mind flies there in an instant and for a moment he feels as if he is home. However, the memory quickly brings him back to his grim reality on the island, and this contrast between his imagination and the truth fills him with despair.
10.What do you think is the overall tone (feel) of the poem?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The overall tone of the poem is one of deep loneliness and longing, mixed with a faint thread of resignation and hope. Selkirk laments his isolation but by the final stanza accepts his situation, finding small comfort in the idea that “there's mercy in every place.”
Section 2 — Prose Comprehension  [20 marks]
Read the extract below carefully and answer Questions 11–20. Each question is worth 2 marks. Use evidence from the text in your answers.

Birds Every Child Should Know

by Neltje Blanchan
If all his lessons were as joyful as learning to know the birds in the fields and woods, there would be no “…whining Schoole-boy with his Satchell / And shining morning face creeping like Snaile / Unwillingly to schoole.”
Long before his nine o'clock headache appears, lessons have begun. Nature herself is the teacher who rouses him from his bed with an outburst of song under the window and sets his sleepy brain to wondering whether it was a robin's clear, ringing call that startled him from his dreams, or the chipping sparrow's wiry tremulo, or the gushing little wren's tripping cadenza. Interest in the birds trains the ear quite unconsciously. A keen, intelligent listener is rare, even among grown-ups, but a child who is becoming acquainted with the birds about him hears every sound and puzzles out its meaning with a cleverness that amazes those with ears who hear not. He responds to the first alarm note from the nesting blue birds in the orchard and dashes out of the house to chase away a prowling cat. He knows from afar the distress caws of a company of crows and away he goes to be sure that their persecutor is a hawk. A faint tattoo in the woods sends him climbing up a tall straight tree with the confident expectation of finding a woodpecker's nest within the hole in its side.
While training his ears, Nature is also training every muscle in his body, sending him on long tramps across the fields in pursuit of a new bird to be identified, making him run and jump fences and wade brooks and climb trees with the zest that produces an appetite like a saw-mill's and deep sleep at the close of a happy day.
When President Roosevelt was a boy he was far from strong, and his anxious father and mother naturally encouraged every interest that he showed in out-of-door pleasures. Among these, perhaps the keenest that he had was in birds. He knew the haunts of every species within a wide radius of his home and made a large collection of eggs and skins that he presented to the Smithsonian Museum when he could no longer endure the evidences of his “youthful indiscretion.” But those bird hunts that had kept him happily employed in the open air all day long, helped to make him the strong, manly man he became. No one abhors the killing of birds and the robbing of nests more than he; few men, not specialists, know so much about bird life.
Nature, the best teacher of us all, trains the child's eyes through study of the birds to quickness and precision, which are the first requisites for all intelligent observation. What does the study of birds do for the imagination, that high power possessed by humans alone, that lifts them upward step by step into new realms of discovery and joy? If the thought of a tiny hummingbird migrating from New England to Central America will not stimulate a child's imagination, then all the tales of fairies and giants and beautiful princesses and wicked witches will not cause his sluggish fancy to roam. Poetry and music, too, would fail to stir it out of the deadly commonplace.
Interest in bird life exercises the sympathies. The child reflects something of the joy of the oriole whose ecstasy of song from the elm on the lawn tells the whereabouts of a deeply hidden nest. He takes to heart the tragedy of a robin's nest in the apple tree that was washed apart by a storm, and experiences something akin to remorse when he takes a mother bird from the jaws of his pet cat. He listens for the return of the bluebirds and is strangely excited and happy that bleak day in March when they re-appear. It is nature sympathy, the growth of the heart, not nature study, the training of the brain, that does most for us.
11.Give one example of a simile from the extract and explain why it is effective.[2]

ⓘ Model answer: One simile is “creeping like Snaile / Unwillingly to schoole”, which compares a reluctant schoolboy to a snail. It is effective because it humorously exaggerates the child's slowness and unwillingness to go to school, making the contrast with joyful nature lessons all the stronger.
12.What does the author mean by saying that “lessons begin before nine o'clock”?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The author means that a child who is interested in birds begins learning naturally long before school starts. As soon as the birds wake him with their songs at dawn, he is already using his ears and brain to identify different species — Nature is the teacher, not the classroom.
13.What does “unconsciously” mean in the phrase “trains the ear quite unconsciously”?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: “Unconsciously” means that the child is not aware that learning is happening. He is not deliberately trying to improve his listening skills — he is simply enjoying the birds — yet his ears are being trained without him realising it.
14.Why do you think the author personifies Nature throughout the extract?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: By giving Nature the qualities of a teacher — she “rouses him from his bed” and is “training every muscle” — the author suggests that the natural world is the most effective educator of all. Personification makes Nature seem caring and purposeful, elevating it above any classroom teacher.
15.Why did President Roosevelt's parents encourage him to pursue outdoor activities when he was a child?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: Roosevelt “was far from strong” as a boy, so his parents were anxious about his health and encouraged any interest in outdoor activities, hoping the fresh air and exercise would make him physically stronger. Bird-watching in particular kept him active all day.
16.Give an example of personification from the extract and explain its effect.[2]

ⓘ Model answer: “Nature herself is the teacher who rouses him from his bed” personifies Nature as a schoolteacher who actively wakes the child. This is effective because it makes natural learning seem as intentional and valuable as formal education, whilst also suggesting it is far more enjoyable.
17.“Many there are who have eyes and see not.” What does this mean?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: This means that although most people have the ability to see, they do not truly observe or notice the world around them. Unlike a child trained by watching birds, most people look but do not pay real attention to what is in front of them.
18.According to the author, what do humans possess that no other living thing does?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The author states that humans alone possess “the imagination, that high power… that lifts them upward step by step into new realms of discovery and joy.” The ability to imagine is presented as uniquely human.
19.How does studying birds benefit a child's physical fitness, according to the extract?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: Following birds forces a child to go on “long tramps across the fields”, run, jump fences, wade streams and climb trees. This vigorous exercise trains “every muscle in his body” and gives him a healthy appetite and deep sleep — as shown by Roosevelt, who became physically strong through his outdoor bird-watching.
20.What is the main point made in the final paragraph?[2]

ⓘ Model answer: The final paragraph argues that studying birds develops a child's emotional life — their sympathy and capacity for feeling. The author concludes that “nature sympathy, the growth of the heart” is more valuable than mere intellectual “nature study.” It is caring about the natural world, not just knowing facts about it, that truly benefits us.
