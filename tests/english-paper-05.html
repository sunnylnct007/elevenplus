The Spirit of the Fair

an original story
Priya has practised her baking for weeks. Today she will enter the village fair's baking competition for the very first time, and her nerves are almost too much to bear.
1.The morning of the village fair, Priya woke long before the sun. Her stomach fluttered like a jar of trapped moths, and she lay very still, listening to the rain drumming softly on the roof. Today she would enter the baking 5.competition for the very first time. ‘You have practised for weeks,’ her grandmother had promised the night before. ‘Trust your hands, and trust yourself.’ Yet as Priya tiptoed downstairs, her confidence drained out of her like water through a sieve.
10.The village hall smelled of cinnamon, warm sugar and nervous excitement. Long tables stretched from wall to wall, crowded with older bakers who chatted easily and measured their flour without so much as a glance. Priya set out her ingredients with 15.trembling fingers. What if everyone laughed? What if her cake was the worst in the whole room? She almost turned around and walked straight back out of the door.
Somehow she stayed. She whisked and folded and poured, and slid her tin into the oven. But when the 20.moment came to lift her sponge onto the plate, disaster struck. The cake sagged in the middle, then collapsed entirely, spilling crumbs across the table like a tiny landslide. Priya’s eyes stung. ‘I give up,’ she whispered.
25.A gentle voice spoke beside her. ‘A fallen cake is not a failure,’ said Mrs Okafor, the kindly judge. ‘It is only a beginning.’ Something in those words steadied Priya. She breathed deeply, gathered the ruined sponge into a bowl, and began again, folding in berries, cream and a 30.fistful of courage. This time her hands did not shake.
When the judges tasted her second attempt, they paused, whispered and smiled. Priya had not won first prize; a towering lemon gateau took 35.that. But Mrs Okafor pressed a bright ribbon into her palm. ‘The Spirit of the Fair,’ she announced warmly, ‘for the baker who refused to give up.’ Priya’s cheeks glowed like the ribbon. She had learned something no recipe could teach: that courage, not perfection, is the finest 40.ingredient of all.
