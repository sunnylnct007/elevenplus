The Last Keeper

An original passage for 11+ practice
1.The summer Maya turned eleven, her family rented a cottage at the edge of Dunmore Cove. The village was small and salt-stained, its streets no wider than a whisper, and it smelled of rope and fish and possibility. On the third morning, restless while her parents 5.slept, Maya followed a gull along the clifftop path until the path ended abruptly at a rusted iron gate.
Beyond the gate stood the lighthouse. It rose from the headland like a pale finger pointing at the sky, its whitewashed walls blotched with orange lichen and patches of old mortar. A brass lantern, tarnished but intact, crowned its summit. 10.Maya had never seen anything that looked simultaneously so proud and so neglected. She pushed the gate, which swung open with a groan of protest, and picked her way across the uneven courtyard. A wooden door stood ajar. She knocked.
“Come in, then,” said a voice like gravel stirred with a spoon. “I saw you from the lamp room.” 15.The old man sitting by the window was as weathered as driftwood, his white beard clipped close and his hands — enormous, rope-scarred hands — wrapped around a mug of tea. He introduced himself as Arthur Aldridge, keeper of the light for forty-seven years.
“But the light doesn’t work any more,” said Maya, glancing up at the dormant lantern. 20.“No,” he agreed, without sadness. “GPS and satellite have made me redundant. But they were not always here.” He leaned forward, and his pale blue eyes caught the morning light. “In November of 1987, a container vessel named the Calliope was driven onto the rocks in a force-nine gale. I kept the light burning for eleven 25.hours, rotating it by hand when the mechanism failed. The captain told me afterwards that my beam was the only fixed point in a world of chaos.”
Maya sat without being invited. Outside, the sea rolled and glittered beneath a sky of fragile blue. She did not want to leave. Arthur smiled slowly, as if unfolding 30.something he had kept pressed between the pages of memory. “There was a fishing smack in March of 1974,” he began, “crewed by three brothers from this very village. The youngest was not yet sixteen.”
He talked for over an hour. He spoke of merchant ships and rescue boats, of fog so thick it had weight, of the peculiar silence that settled over the sea between 35.gales. Maya listened, her knees drawn up to her chest on the low wooden stool, occasionally asking a question that made Arthur pause and smile, as if surprised to be understood.
When her mother’s voice finally drifted up from the village path below, Maya rose reluctantly. 40.“Will you come back?” the old man asked. She looked at him, at the tarnished lamp above, at the sea stretching outward in every direction with its cargo of stories she had not yet heard.
“Every day,” she promised.
